# Day Trading — Complete Guide

## What Day Trading Is
Day trading means opening and closing all positions within the same trading day — no overnight holds. The goal: capture intraday price movements. Requires active monitoring during market hours. The most demanding trading style psychologically and technically.

**Pattern Day Trader Rule:** In the US, if you execute 4 or more day trades in a 5-business-day period in a margin account, you are classified as a Pattern Day Trader and need at least $25,000 in your account. This is a hard regulatory requirement for US stock traders.

## The Most Important Pre-Market Habit

**30 minutes before market open:**
1. Check overnight futures (S&P 500 futures, Nasdaq futures)
2. Check major news (CNBC, Bloomberg) for market-moving events
3. Pull up the economic calendar — any high-impact events today?
4. Check your watchlist for earnings, FDA announcements, major news
5. Identify the pre-market high and low for each stock you are watching
6. Mark the previous day's high and low on every chart
7. Calculate the expected gap for any gapping stocks

This preparation is not optional — it is what separates disciplined day traders from reactive ones.

## The Most Reliable Day Trading Setups

### Setup 1: Opening Range Breakout (ORB)
The opening range is the high and low established in the first 5, 15, or 30 minutes of trading.

**The logic:** The first 5-30 minutes represents the initial battle between buyers and sellers after the overnight pause. When price breaks above the range high, buyers have won the battle — typically with momentum continuation. When it breaks below the low, sellers have won.

**How to trade:**
1. Mark the high and low of the first 5 minutes (or 15 minutes) on your chart
2. Watch for a clean break with volume
3. Entry: Break above ORB high (long) or below ORB low (short)
4. Stop: Below the ORB low (if long) or above ORB high (if short)
5. Target: Previous day's high (if long) or previous day's low (if short)

**Best conditions:** Strong pre-market catalyst (earnings beat, positive news), gap up that holds above the previous close, and expanding volume on the breakout.

**Avoid:** Opening range breakouts on low-volume market open days with no catalyst. These fail frequently — the break is random, not momentum-driven.

### Setup 2: VWAP Reclaim (High Conviction)
After a stock opens and drops below VWAP, it reclaims VWAP and holds above it on a retest.

**The logic:** VWAP is the institutional benchmark. A stock that trades below VWAP most of the morning and then reclaims it is showing institutional buyers stepping in. The retest of VWAP as support confirms the level.

**How to trade:**
1. Stock gaps up, trades down to or below VWAP
2. Price action holds at VWAP or very slightly below
3. Price bounces and closes a 5-minute candle ABOVE VWAP
4. Price retests VWAP from above and holds (higher low)
5. Entry: Break above the initial recovery candle's high
6. Stop: Below VWAP (the retest low)
7. Target: Previous day's high or VWAP + 2 standard deviations

### Setup 3: High of Day Momentum Break
When a stock makes a new intraday high with accelerating volume.

**How to trade:**
1. Stock has been trending up throughout the morning
2. Creates a consolidation just below a previous intraday high
3. Breaks to a new high of day with clear volume surge
4. Entry: Break above the new high of day
5. Stop: Below the consolidation base just before the break
6. Target: Previous day's high, then round numbers, then next resistance

**Key requirement:** The trend must have been established before this setup. A first-hour HOD break in a strong momentum stock is very different from a random afternoon breakout with no volume.

### Setup 4: Gap and Go
When a stock gaps up significantly at the open AND holds the gap with continued buying pressure.

**Conditions for a legitimate Gap and Go:**
- Strong fundamental catalyst (earnings beat, buyout news, major analyst upgrade)
- Pre-market volume significantly elevated
- Price holding well above the gap level in pre-market
- First 5 minutes: price consolidates above the gap, not immediately selling back

**Entry:** Break above the 5-minute opening range high on volume
**Stop:** Below the pre-market low or back into the gap
**Target:** Previous day's high, then psychological levels above

**The trap:** "Gap and Give Back" — stock gaps up but immediately sells off. This is distribution — the news was already priced in. Never chase a gapping stock that is immediately selling off. The direction of the first 5 minutes after the open is usually the direction of the day.

## Intraday Support and Resistance Levels

Mark these before the market opens every day:

**From yesterday:**
- Previous day's high (PDH): Most important level. Break above = momentum long.
- Previous day's low (PDL): Most important support. Break below = momentum short.
- Previous day's close

**From today:**
- VWAP (recalculated fresh each day)
- Opening range high and low (first 5 or 15 minutes)
- HOD (high of day) and LOD (low of day) — update as the day progresses

**Pre-market levels:**
- Pre-market high: Often significant resistance if price trades back to it
- Pre-market low: Often significant support

## Time of Day Patterns

**9:30-10:30am (The Power Hour):**
The most important 60 minutes of the day. Most volume. Most volatility. Best momentum setups. Most opportunities. Also most risk — news is still processing, spreads are wider early.

**10:30am-12:00pm:**
Momentum continues from the open. Setups from the opening range become clearer. Generally the best time for new intraday entries.

**12:00pm-2:00pm (The Dead Zone):**
Volume drops significantly. Market makers widen spreads. Patterns are less reliable. Many professional day traders do not initiate new positions in this window. Wait for the afternoon session.

**2:00pm-3:00pm:**
Volume picks back up. Often directional moves resume. Fed announcements typically at 2pm cause volatility.

**3:00pm-4:00pm (The Power Hour #2):**
Heavy institutional activity as funds rebalance and close positions. Trends established in the morning often accelerate or reverse. Leveraged ETF rebalancing creates systematic flows. Good momentum trades available but also more reversals.

## Max Daily Loss Rule

Before the day starts, decide: if I lose $X today, I stop trading.

**Why this rule is essential:** Bad trading days breed emotional decisions. The worst trading losses in history occurred when someone who was down significantly tried to "make it back" in the same day. Each additional loss amplified the emotion. The rule forces a full stop before catastrophe.

**Common rule:** Stop trading when you lose 2-3% of account in a single day. Walk away. The market will be there tomorrow.

## News Catalyst Trading

High-impact news creates the best day trading conditions because:
- Volume is massive
- Directional momentum is clear
- Retail traders are caught wrong and forced to cover/chase
- Institutions are repositioning, creating sustained moves

**Types of catalysts:**
- Earnings (pre-market or after-hours release, trading begins at next open)
- FDA drug approval/rejection
- Major analyst upgrades/downgrades with specific price targets
- M&A announcement (acquisition premium)
- Major company news (product launch, lawsuit resolution, executive departure)
- Macro data (CPI, NFP, FOMC decision)

**Trading the catalyst correctly:**
Do NOT buy the news headline automatically. Wait for the first 5-minute candle to close. See which direction the smart money is taking it. Then trade with the direction, not your prediction.
