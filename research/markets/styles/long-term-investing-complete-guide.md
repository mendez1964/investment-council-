# Long-Term Investing — Complete Guide

## What Long-Term Investing Is
Long-term investing holds positions for months to years (or indefinitely for the best businesses). This is the domain of the Buffett, Lynch, Dalio, and Graham frameworks. The goal: own excellent businesses or assets during their periods of compounding value creation.

---

## The Core Framework: What to Own Long-Term

**Excellent businesses (Buffett/Lynch approach):**
- Durable competitive advantage (moat)
- Management that allocates capital intelligently
- Consistent high returns on equity (15%+)
- Free cash flow generation that grows over time
- Understandable business model
- Reasonable valuation relative to intrinsic value

**Diversified all-weather allocation (Dalio approach):**
- Exposure to multiple economic environments
- Balance between growth assets (stocks) and safe assets (bonds, gold)
- No single position that can destroy the portfolio
- Rebalancing when allocations drift significantly

---

## Dollar Cost Averaging (DCA)

For long-term investors who want to build positions over time:

**What it is:** Investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals (monthly, quarterly) regardless of price.

**Why it works:** You automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high. Over time, this averages out to a favorable cost basis. It removes the psychological pressure of trying to time the market perfectly.

**The math:** $500/month invested in an asset that sometimes costs $25 and sometimes costs $50 buys you more at $25 and less at $50. Over time, your average cost is lower than the average price.

**Best used for:** Index funds, Bitcoin, gold, and any high-conviction long-term holding where you believe in the thesis but cannot predict short-term price movements.

## When to Add vs. When to Hold

**When to add to a long-term position:**
- Price has pulled back significantly (15%+) from a high without the fundamental story changing
- Valuation has become more attractive (PE compressed, price-to-book fallen)
- Insider buying appears (management is telling you with their money)
- The macro environment is more supportive than when you last bought
- The original thesis has strengthened, not weakened

**When to hold without adding:**
- Price has risen significantly but the story is still intact
- Valuation is reasonable but not cheap
- You have conviction in the business but not a compelling reason to add risk

**When to sell a long-term holding (hardest decision):**
- The fundamental story has changed (moat is eroding, management quality declined)
- Valuation is extreme — priced for perfection with no margin of safety
- Better opportunity elsewhere that offers higher conviction at better value
- You were wrong about the original thesis — recognize it and move on

## Rebalancing

Long-term portfolios drift over time. A position that doubles in a diversified portfolio now represents twice as much of the portfolio as intended.

**When to rebalance:**
- When any single position exceeds 20-25% of portfolio (for stock investors)
- Annually as a baseline practice
- When the overall asset allocation drifts 5+ percentage points from target (for all-weather style)

**Rebalancing method:** Sell a portion of over-weight positions and add to under-weight positions. This systematically forces you to "sell high and buy low" without making active predictions.

## Surviving Drawdowns

The greatest enemy of long-term investors is panic selling during market corrections.

**Historical context to remember:**
- The S&P 500 has declined 10%+ in most years at some point
- The S&P 500 has declined 20%+ approximately every 3-4 years
- The S&P 500 has never failed to make new highs eventually after every decline
- Every bear market that felt like the end of the world turned out to be a buying opportunity

**Practical rules for drawdowns:**
1. Do not look at your portfolio daily during a major sell-off — it causes emotional decisions
2. Have a written investment thesis for each position — refer to it during corrections
3. Ask "has anything changed fundamentally" before selling — if no, the correct response to a drawdown is often nothing
4. If position size becomes uncomfortably large relative to portfolio during a decline, trim to sleeping size — not zero
5. Use dollar cost averaging into the drawdown if conviction remains high

**Buffett's perspective:** He views market crashes as sales at the department store. The same business he loved at $50 is even more attractive at $35. Lower price equals better value, assuming nothing fundamental changed.

## Long-Term Crypto Allocation

For long-term investors who accept Bitcoin and Ethereum as portfolio components:

**The thesis for long-term crypto:**
- Digital scarcity (fixed supply of 21 million Bitcoin ever)
- Censorship-resistant store of value
- Hedge against fiat currency debasement
- Dalio and Tudor Jones have both made this argument publicly

**Allocation sizing:**
- Conservative: 1-3% of portfolio
- Moderate: 5-10% of portfolio
- Never more than you can watch decline 80% without panicking

**Storage:** For significant long-term holdings, cold storage (hardware wallet) eliminates exchange counterparty risk.

**DCA approach:** Monthly purchases of a fixed dollar amount smooths volatility over multi-year holding periods.
