# Elliott Wave Theory — Complete Reference

## What Elliott Wave Is
Developed by Ralph Nelson Elliott in the 1930s. Elliott observed that stock markets move in recognizable, repetitive wave patterns that reflect the natural rhythm of crowd psychology. The waves occur on every scale — from a single hour of trading to decades of market history.

---

## The Basic Structure

### The Impulse Wave (5 Waves in the Direction of Trend)
Markets trend in 5 waves in the primary direction:
- Wave 1: Initial move in new direction
- Wave 2: Correction of Wave 1 (retracement)
- Wave 3: Strongest and longest move
- Wave 4: Correction of Wave 3
- Wave 5: Final move, often with divergence

### The Corrective Wave (3 Waves Against the Trend)
After the 5-wave impulse, the market corrects in 3 waves:
- Wave A: First leg of correction
- Wave B: Counter-rally within the correction
- Wave C: Final leg of correction, completing the full cycle

**The complete cycle:** 5 waves up, 3 waves down = 8 waves total. Then the next 5-3 cycle begins at a larger scale.

## Wave Characteristics in Detail

### Wave 1
Often the hardest to identify in real-time because it looks like just another bounce in the existing trend.
- Volume: Moderate. Not yet the high-conviction surge.
- News: Still predominantly negative (if starting from a bear market bottom)
- Fibonacci: Often equals the first impulse from the prior trend's Wave 5 bottom
- Common mistake: Selling this rally, thinking it is just a dead-cat bounce

### Wave 2
A retracement of Wave 1. Often deep — can retrace 61.8% or more of Wave 1.
- Does NOT create a new price extreme (cannot go below Wave 1's origin)
- Volume: Diminishing on the decline — sellers are losing momentum
- Sentiment: Fear, disbelief, skepticism about the rally
- The "buy" opportunity: Late cycle fear creates a second chance entry before Wave 3
- Common characteristic: Often ends on a three-wave corrective pattern itself

### Wave 3 — The Power Wave
The longest, strongest, and most rewarding wave. This is where the trend becomes obvious.
- Volume: Highest of all waves. Expanding throughout.
- News: Fundamentals start to improve, analysts upgrade, media covers the move
- Price: Strong, continuous, few meaningful pullbacks
- Fibonacci: Typically extends to 161.8% or 261.8% of Wave 1
- The rule: Wave 3 CANNOT be the shortest impulse wave
- If you can only catch one wave, catch Wave 3

**How to identify you are in Wave 3:** Previous Wave 1 high is broken decisively on expanding volume. The move accelerates. Every pullback is small and immediately bought.

### Wave 4
A consolidation or correction after Wave 3.
- The rule: Wave 4 CANNOT overlap with Wave 1's price territory
- This is the alternation principle at work with Wave 2
- Volume: Declining during the correction — signals it is corrective, not the beginning of a bear market
- Often less deep than Wave 2 (alternation rule — if Wave 2 was deep, Wave 4 is shallow)
- Typically retraces 38.2% or less of Wave 3

**Alternation principle:** Wave 2 and Wave 4 tend to alternate in character. If Wave 2 was a sharp, deep correction, Wave 4 tends to be a flat, sideways consolidation. And vice versa. This helps forecast what kind of correction is coming.

### Wave 5
The final wave in the impulse sequence.
- Volume: Often LESS than Wave 3 — the move is getting tired
- Momentum: RSI and MACD often show bearish divergence vs Wave 3 highs
- Sentiment: Maximum bullishness — everyone is confident, the rally seems unstoppable
- Often equals Wave 1 in price length
- Sometimes fails to reach Wave 3's level (truncation) — a very bearish sign
- This is where to take profits from Wave 3 longs, not to initiate new longs

## The Three Inviolable Rules

These rules cannot be broken. If your wave count violates any of these, the count is wrong.

**Rule 1:** Wave 2 never retraces more than 100% of Wave 1. (It cannot go to a new extreme below Wave 1's start in an uptrend.)

**Rule 2:** Wave 3 is never the shortest impulse wave. It must be longer than either Wave 1 or Wave 5 (or both).

**Rule 3:** Wave 4 never overlaps with Wave 1's price territory. The end of Wave 4 cannot enter the range of Wave 1.

## Corrective Patterns (ABC)

### Simple Zigzag (ABC)
The most common corrective pattern.
- Wave A: Sharp decline (often 5 sub-waves)
- Wave B: Rally of approximately 50-61.8% of Wave A
- Wave C: Sharp decline, often equaling Wave A in length
The correction ends at the terminus of Wave C.

### Flat Correction
- Wave A: Modest decline
- Wave B: Rally that retraces most or all of Wave A (nearly back to the prior high)
- Wave C: Decline that roughly equals Wave A
This creates a sideways, choppy correction — frustrating to trade.

### Triangle Correction
Five overlapping waves creating converging trendlines. The most complex corrective pattern.
- Appears most commonly in Wave 4 or Wave B position
- Eventually resolves with a "thrust" in the direction of the primary trend
- The thrust after a triangle is often sharp and fast

## Wave Degrees — The Fractal Nature

Elliott Wave is fractal — the same 5-3 pattern exists at every scale simultaneously.

From largest to smallest:
- Grand Supercycle: Multi-century (100+ years)
- Supercycle: Multi-decade (40-70 years)
- Cycle: Decade (8-40 years)
- Primary: Years (1-4 years)
- Intermediate: Months (3-13 months)
- Minor: Weeks (3-13 weeks)
- Minute: Days
- Minuette: Hours
- Sub-Minuette: Minutes

**What this means practically:** A 5-wave sequence up on the daily chart is Wave 1 of a larger Wave 1 on the weekly chart. Each move is simultaneously an end in itself and a component of a larger pattern.

**Practical application:** When your daily chart shows a Wave 3 within a larger weekly Wave 3, you have maximum confirmation of trend strength — the small-scale and large-scale patterns are aligned.

## Using Fibonacci to Confirm Wave Counts

Fibonacci ratios are the mathematical backbone of Elliott Wave.

**Expected Fibonacci relationships:**
- Wave 2 retraces 38.2%, 50%, or 61.8% of Wave 1
- Wave 3 extends to 161.8% or 261.8% of Wave 1
- Wave 4 retraces 23.6% or 38.2% of Wave 3
- Wave 5 often equals Wave 1 (100% relationship)
- Wave B of a zigzag retraces 50-61.8% of Wave A
- Wave C often equals Wave A

When your wave count has Fibonacci confluence — wave 3 projected target matches a key Fibonacci extension that has been achieved — you have high confidence the count is correct.

## Common Elliott Wave Mistakes

**1. Over-counting:** Seeing waves everywhere in random noise. Elliott Wave patterns require clear impulse and correction structure. Not every price move qualifies.

**2. Under-labeling corrections:** Corrections are often complex and confusing. Do not force simple counts onto complex patterns.

**3. Ignoring the rules:** If your count violates one of the three rules, the count is wrong — revise it, do not rationalize it.

**4. Trading Wave 5 like Wave 3:** Wave 5 is the final wave. Using it to initiate large new positions is dangerous — the correction (ABC) follows.

**5. Not using alternative counts:** Always have an alternative count ready. If your primary count is invalidated, the alternative is your backup plan.

## Practical Trading Application

**Best entry:** Buy the Wave 2 correction after confirming Wave 1, targeting Wave 3. The risk is tight (stop below Wave 1 origin) and the reward is large (Wave 3 is the biggest move).

**Best hold:** Position through Wave 3. Do not sell just because the move feels extended — Wave 3 is supposed to feel extended.

**Best exit:** Take significant profits at the projected Wave 3 target (161.8% extension of Wave 1). Reduce risk going into Wave 5.

**Best short:** After Wave 5 completes with bearish divergence in RSI/MACD, position for the ABC correction. Target Wave A = Wave C relationship.
