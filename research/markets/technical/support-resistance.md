# Support and Resistance — Complete Reference

## The Core Concept
Support is a price level where buying pressure has been strong enough to stop a decline. Resistance is a price level where selling pressure has been strong enough to stop an advance. These are the most fundamental concepts in all of technical analysis.

Support and resistance exist because of human memory. Traders remember prices where they bought or sold in the past and make decisions at those same levels again in the future.

---

## Types of Support and Resistance

### 1. Horizontal Price Levels
The most important type. Previous highs and lows leave a memory in the market.

**Previous highs become resistance:** If a stock reached $50 and fell back, there are traders who bought near $50 who are sitting on losses. When price returns to $50 they will sell to break even — creating supply at that level.

**Previous lows become support:** If a stock found a bottom at $30 and rallied, there are traders who missed the rally watching for a chance to buy. When price returns to $30 they buy — creating demand at that level.

**The flip:** This is the most powerful concept in support/resistance. When a support level is broken decisively, it becomes resistance. When a resistance level is broken decisively, it becomes support. The old floor becomes the new ceiling and vice versa.

*Example:* Stock trades between $40 (support) and $50 (resistance) for months. It breaks below $40 with strong volume. $40 is now resistance — every rally toward $40 will be sold by former buyers who are now trying to recover losses. The previous floor is now the ceiling.

### 2. Round Numbers (Psychological Levels)
Humans naturally think in round numbers. $50, $100, $200, $500, $1000 are all psychological support/resistance levels in stocks. $20,000, $50,000, $100,000 in Bitcoin. These levels attract both buyers and sellers before they are tested.

**Why they work:** Fund managers set price targets in round numbers. Retail traders set stop losses at round numbers. Options market makers have the highest open interest at round strikes. The concentration of orders at these levels creates self-fulfilling support and resistance.

### 3. Gap Fills
When price opens significantly above or below the previous close, it leaves a gap — a price range where no trading occurred. Gaps act as support (gap up) or resistance (gap down) until they are filled.

**Full gap fills:** Markets tend to eventually fill most gaps. A gap up that later gets filled means price returns to the level just before the gap.

**The gap support rule:** An unfilled gap above price often acts as support when price pulls back to it. The logic: buyers who missed the initial gap are waiting for price to return so they can buy. This demand creates support.

### 4. VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)
The average price weighted by volume for the trading day. The most important intraday support/resistance level.

**Why VWAP matters:** Institutional buyers and sellers benchmark their execution against VWAP. Many algorithms are programmed to buy below VWAP and sell above it. This creates a gravitational pull.

**Intraday rules:**
- Price above VWAP = buyers have control of the day, look for long setups
- Price below VWAP = sellers have control of the day, look for short setups
- Price at VWAP = battle zone, wait for direction to be established
- First touch of VWAP after a strong move often provides a trade

**Anchored VWAP:** VWAP calculated from a specific starting date (an earnings report, a significant pivot point). Powerful support/resistance at key institutional benchmarks.

### 5. Moving Averages as Dynamic Support/Resistance
Moving averages move with price — they are dynamic support and resistance levels that shift over time.

**The key moving averages:**
- 9 EMA: Day trading support/resistance. Fast, responsive.
- 20 EMA: Swing trading support. In an uptrend, pullbacks to 20 EMA are often buy opportunities.
- 50 SMA: Intermediate support. Major test level. Stocks in healthy uptrends find buyers at the 50 SMA.
- 200 SMA: The bull/bear dividing line. Above 200 SMA = long-term uptrend. Below = long-term downtrend. Crosses of the 200 SMA are major signals.

**The moving average flip:** Same principle as horizontal levels. A rising 50 SMA that price breaks below becomes resistance on the way back up. A falling 50 SMA that price breaks above becomes support.

### 6. Trendlines
Connect two or more significant lows (uptrend line) or two or more significant highs (downtrend line). These angled levels of support/resistance represent the slope of the trend itself.

**Drawing trendlines correctly:**
- Use significant highs and lows — not just any price point
- At least two touches are needed to draw a valid line, three touches confirm it
- The angle matters — a 45-degree trend is ideal; too steep means unsustainable
- The more touches, the stronger the line — and the more significant the eventual break

**The trendline break:** When price breaks through a trendline on volume, it signals a change in the rate of change of the trend — not necessarily a reversal, but a meaningful shift. After a break, the trendline often becomes resistance (if broken downward) or support (if broken upward).

### 7. Fibonacci Levels
Based on the Fibonacci sequence and the golden ratio (0.618). Price retracements frequently find support or resistance at these mathematically-derived levels.

**The key Fibonacci retracement levels from a move:**
- 23.6%: Shallow retracement — trend is very strong if support holds here
- 38.2%: Moderate retracement — healthy pullback in a trend
- 50.0%: Psychological midpoint — not a Fibonacci number but widely watched
- 61.8%: The golden ratio — the most important Fibonacci level; deep retracement but often holds in strong trends
- 78.6%: Very deep retracement — trend in question if price falls this far

**Extension levels for targets:**
- 127.2%: First extension target
- 161.8%: Primary extension target
- 261.8%: Extended target for powerful trends

**How to draw correctly:** Identify a significant swing low and swing high (or high and low for a downtrend). Apply the Fibonacci tool from one extreme to the other. The percentage levels become the support/resistance grid.

### 8. Volume Profile (Point of Control)
Shows the distribution of volume at different price levels over a period. The Point of Control (POC) is the price level with the highest volume traded — the most accepted price. The Value Area is the range containing 70% of all volume traded.

**Why it matters:** High volume nodes act as support and resistance because the most transactions occurred there. People bought and sold most heavily at those prices — they remember them.

**Low volume nodes (gaps in volume):** Price tends to move quickly through areas where little volume was traded — no memory there. These are areas of least resistance.

## The More Times a Level Is Tested, the Weaker It Becomes

This is counterintuitive but important. When price tests the same support level repeatedly, each test absorbs some of the buying power at that level. Eventually the pool of buyers at that level is exhausted.

**Practical implication:** A first test of major support is usually the strongest — the best opportunity to buy. A fifth test of the same support level may be the last — the level may be about to break.

**The rule:** Buy first tests of key support. Be cautious on fourth and fifth tests of the same level.

---

## How to Draw Levels Correctly

**The wrong way:** Drawing exact lines at the precise high or low of a wick. Wicks represent emotion — they get violated easily.

**The right way:** Draw zones, not lines. A support or resistance zone is a price range approximately 0.5-1% wide. Price does not stop on a dime — it finds areas of support or resistance.

**Prioritization:** Not all levels are equal.
1. Monthly/weekly chart levels: Most significant — watched by everyone from hedge funds to retail
2. Daily chart levels: Significant — watched by swing traders and institutions
3. Intraday levels: Short-term significance only

**Multi-timeframe confluence:** When a level appears as support on the weekly chart AND coincides with the 200 SMA AND is at a Fibonacci retracement level — that is a zone of maximum confluence. These are the highest-probability trade locations.

## Trading the Retest

One of the highest-probability setups in trading:
1. Price breaks above resistance (or below support) decisively
2. Price pulls back to the broken level
3. The broken level holds as new support (or resistance) on the retest
4. Entry on the retest, stop below the level, target the next significant level

**Why it works:** The break confirmed buyers are stronger than sellers. The retest gives late buyers and early profit-takers a second chance to position. If the level holds on the retest, the thesis is confirmed with much less risk than chasing the initial break.
