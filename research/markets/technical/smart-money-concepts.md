# Smart Money Concepts (SMC) — Comprehensive Methodology Guide

## Overview

Smart Money Concepts (SMC) is a trading methodology built on the premise that financial markets are not random but are instead engineered by large institutional participants — banks, hedge funds, and market makers — who systematically extract liquidity from retail traders before executing their true directional moves. SMC reframes traditional technical analysis (support, resistance, breakouts) as mechanisms of deception, and teaches traders to align with institutional order flow rather than fight it.

The methodology draws heavily from the work of ICT (Inner Circle Trader, the alias of Michael J. Huddleston), whose publicly released concepts from the early 2010s became widely adopted and repackaged under the SMC umbrella. It has since become one of the most-discussed retail trading frameworks, particularly in forex, indices, and crypto markets.

---

## Core Philosophy: Institutional Order Flow

### Why Retail Traders Lose

The SMC framework begins with a structural critique of traditional retail technical analysis. The argument: retail traders cluster stop losses and pending orders at predictable locations — above swing highs, below swing lows, at round numbers, and at "obvious" support/resistance levels. This concentration of resting orders creates **liquidity pools** that institutions require to fill their own large orders without excessive slippage.

When a bank needs to buy hundreds of millions of dollars of an asset, it cannot simply place a market order — the impact would move price against it. Instead, the institutional model (as theorized by SMC) involves:

1. **Accumulation:** Quietly building positions in a range, often appearing as consolidation.
2. **Manipulation:** Engineering a move to sweep liquidity (stop hunts), triggering retail stops and pending orders, which provides the counterparty volume needed.
3. **Distribution / True Move:** Executing the intended directional move once sufficient liquidity has been consumed.

This three-phase model (sometimes labeled **AMD — Accumulation, Manipulation, Distribution**) is the backbone of all SMC trade logic.

### Market Maker Model

SMC practitioners view price action through the lens of a market maker who profits from the spread and from positioning against the losing side of retail flow. The implication: wherever retail traders are positioned in mass, the market is likely to move against them first before continuing in the fundamental direction.

## Order Blocks

Order blocks are the foundational trade entry tool in SMC. An **order block** is the last bearish candle before a significant bullish move, or the last bullish candle before a significant bearish move. The logic: this candle represents the zone where institutional orders were placed, and price is likely to return to that zone to fill remaining unfilled orders before continuing the trend.

### Bullish Order Blocks

A bullish order block is identified by:
- A bearish (down) candle or sequence of candles immediately before a strong impulsive move upward
- The move upward must break a prior swing high (structural significance is required)
- The order block zone is defined by the high and low of that last bearish candle (some practitioners use only the body, others include the full wick range)

**Mitigation:** When price retraces into the order block zone, it is said to "mitigate" the block — institutional orders left resting there are filled. A mitigated order block is no longer considered valid for future entries.

**Entry Logic:** Traders look for long entries when price returns to a bullish order block, with stop losses placed below the order block low, targeting the next significant swing high or FVG above.

### Bearish Order Blocks

The inverse: the last bullish candle before a strong impulsive move downward. Entry logic is a short position when price retraces into the zone, stop above the order block high.

### Order Block Quality Factors

Not all order blocks carry equal weight. Higher-quality blocks share these characteristics:
- Created on higher timeframes (4H, daily)
- Associated with a displacement candle (large-bodied, aggressive move away)
- Located at a confluence with other SMC concepts (FVGs, liquidity sweeps, premium/discount zones)
- Untested (unmitigated)

## Fair Value Gaps (FVG) / Imbalances

A **Fair Value Gap** is a three-candle pattern where the middle candle moves so aggressively that the wicks of the first and third candles do not overlap, leaving a price range that was never traded. This gap represents an **imbalance** in buying and selling pressure — price moved through the zone so fast that the market did not have an opportunity to establish fair value.

### FVG Identification

On a candlestick chart, identify:
- Candle 1: its high (for bullish FVG) or low (for bearish FVG)
- Candle 3: its low (for bullish FVG) or high (for bearish FVG)
- The gap between candle 1's high and candle 3's low = the bullish FVG zone

A **bullish FVG** is created during a strong upward move. A **bearish FVG** is created during a strong downward move.

### Price and Gap Fills

Markets have a strong statistical tendency to return and "fill" or partially fill FVGs. This behavior is attributed to:
- Institutional algorithms seeking price efficiency
- Resting orders within the gap zone being triggered
- Market makers balancing the inefficiency

FVGs serve dual roles in SMC: as **retracement targets** (price fills the gap before continuing) and as **reversal zones** (price reacts at the gap edge and resumes trend direction).

The 50% point of the FVG is often called the **Equilibrium** of the gap and is considered the highest-probability reaction zone within it.

## Breaker Blocks

A **breaker block** is a failed order block — it represents a polarity shift and is one of the more nuanced SMC concepts.

### Identification

A breaker forms when:
1. A valid order block exists (bullish or bearish)
2. Price sweeps through the order block, invalidating it (liquidity taken)
3. Price reverses aggressively in the opposite direction

The original order block now **flips its polarity** — a failed bullish order block becomes a bearish breaker, and vice versa. This zone is expected to act as resistance on any subsequent retest.

### Logic Behind Breakers

The reasoning: the institutional orders within the original order block were consumed (mitigated) by the sweep. The new directional move represents a shift in institutional intent. Any traders who bought the bullish order block are now trapped, and their stop losses become fuel for the new downward move. The breaker zone is where those trapped traders exit, providing additional momentum.

Breakers are considered high-probability setups precisely because they represent confirmed structural shifts, not just reversals.

## Liquidity Concepts

Liquidity is perhaps the most central concept in SMC. The core idea: price moves to where orders are clustered, not necessarily to levels of traditional supply and demand.

### Liquidity Pools

**Equal highs** and **equal lows** are high-priority liquidity targets. When price creates two or more highs at approximately the same level, retail traders widely place buy stops just above that level (anticipating a breakout) or stop losses there (for short positions). This creates a dense pool of resting orders.

SMC practitioners watch these levels as likely targets for price to sweep before reversing or continuing.

### Stop Hunts

A **stop hunt** is a deliberate (in the SMC framework) or emergent move designed to trigger clustered stop orders. Characteristics:
- A quick, aggressive spike through a significant level
- Followed immediately by a reversal back through the level
- Often accompanied by a wick on the candle rather than a full body close through the level
- Frequently occurs during high-liquidity sessions or just before/after major news events

Stop hunts are not viewed as random in SMC — they are interpreted as institutional accumulation or distribution events, providing the counterparty flow needed to fill large positions.

### Liquidity Sweeps

A **liquidity sweep** (also called a **liquidity raid**) is a stop hunt at a key structural level. The identification criteria are the same as a stop hunt, but the emphasis is on the structural significance of the level being swept. A sweep of a prior swing high combined with a bearish displacement candle is a high-probability short signal in the SMC framework.

## Change of Character (CHoCH) and Break of Structure (BOS)

These two concepts define how SMC practitioners read market structure shifts.

### Break of Structure (BOS)

A BOS occurs when price takes out a prior significant swing high (in an uptrend) or swing low (in a downtrend), confirming trend continuation. BOS is a **trend-following** signal — it confirms that the prevailing structure remains intact. Traders use BOS confirmation before entering in the direction of the trend.

### Change of Character (CHoCH)

A CHoCH is the first sign of a potential trend reversal. In an uptrend, a CHoCH occurs when price, for the first time, breaks below a prior swing low. This signals that institutional interest may be shifting. Key distinction from BOS: CHoCH is a **counter-trend warning signal**, not an entry in itself, but it prompts traders to begin looking for reversal setups on lower timeframes.

The hierarchy: CHoCH alerts to the possibility of reversal; a subsequent BOS in the new direction confirms it.

## Premium and Discount Zones

SMC uses the **Fibonacci equilibrium** (50% level) of a defined price range to classify the market into two halves:

- **Premium zone:** Upper 50% of the range — where smart money sells
- **Discount zone:** Lower 50% of the range — where smart money buys

The logic: institutions seek to buy at discounted prices and sell at premium prices relative to the established range. Retail traders, influenced by breakout bias, tend to buy highs and sell lows — the opposite of optimal positioning.

**Optimal Trade Entry (OTE):** The zone between the 61.8% and 79% Fibonacci retracement levels of a recent impulse move. This zone sits within the discount zone (for longs) and represents the highest-probability retracement entry, particularly when it aligns with an order block or FVG.

## Internal vs. External Market Structure

SMC differentiates between two structural layers:

**External structure** refers to the major swing highs and lows visible on a higher timeframe chart — the primary trend framework. BOS and CHoCH on external structure carry the most significance.

**Internal structure** refers to the smaller swing points within a larger impulse or correction leg. Internal CHoCH signals precede external structure breaks and serve as early warning indicators for lower timeframe entries aligned with higher timeframe bias.

The multi-timeframe workflow: establish bias on external structure (daily/4H), identify internal structure shifts on lower timeframes (15m/5m) that align with the higher timeframe bias, and enter at an order block or FVG on the lowest timeframe being traded.

## Inducement

**Inducement** is the concept of engineered liquidity designed to trap traders into positions before the real move. The market manufactures a convincing false setup — a clean breakout, a textbook trend continuation — to induce retail entries, accumulate their stop orders as a liquidity pool, and then reverse.

Identifying inducement:
- A move that breaks a minor swing high/low just enough to trigger stops and entries
- Absence of a strong displacement candle on the breakout
- Quickly followed by a reversal through a CHoCH

Inducement is closely related to the stop hunt concept but emphasizes the behavioral element: retail traders are psychologically induced to enter, making their stops predictable.

## Time and Price Theory: Killzones

SMC places significant weight on **when** setups occur. Institutional participation is not uniform across the trading day — it concentrates during specific session windows called **killzones**.

- **Asia Session (Asian Killzone):** Approximately 20:00–00:00 EST. Characterized by lower volume and range-bound price action. Asia often establishes the liquidity (equal highs/lows) that is later raided during London or New York sessions.

- **London Open Killzone:** Approximately 02:00–05:00 EST. One of the highest-probability windows. London is the most liquid forex session and frequently creates the day's directional move, often sweeping Asia session highs or lows in the first hour.

- **New York Open Killzone:** Approximately 07:00–10:00 EST. The second major high-probability window. NYSE open coincides with this period, creating overlapping liquidity with London. The NY open frequently reverses or extends the London move.

- **New York Lunch / PM Session:** Generally avoided in SMC — lower institutional participation, choppy price action with less directional conviction.

Setups forming during killzones at confluent SMC levels (order blocks, FVGs, liquidity sweeps) are assigned the highest probability.

## ICT Methodology Connection

The ICT (Inner Circle Trader) framework is the intellectual origin of most SMC concepts. Michael Huddleston published extensively on concepts including the Judas Swing (false morning move to raid liquidity before true direction), Power of Three (accumulation, manipulation, distribution on intraday timeframes), Optimal Trade Entry, and the concept of a daily/weekly bias derived from prior session liquidity.

SMC as commonly taught today is largely a repackaging and simplification of ICT concepts, with community-developed extensions. Key distinctions: ICT is more prescriptive about specific pip targets and time-based price delivery models (PD Arrays), while SMC in its popular form focuses more broadly on structural concepts applicable across asset classes.

## SMC and Traditional Technical Analysis

SMC does not entirely reject traditional TA but reframes it:

| Traditional TA | SMC Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Support / Resistance | Liquidity pools; likely to be swept before holding |
| Breakout | Potential stop hunt; wait for retest confirmation |
| Trend lines | Retail draws them; institutions raid them |
| Moving averages | Equilibrium reference; not entry signals |
| Volume | Displacement candle body size serves as proxy |

The key synthesis: traditional TA identifies where retail orders cluster; SMC predicts how institutions will exploit those clusters before trending. The most durable SMC setups occur at levels that traditional TA considers "confirmed" — breakouts, trend continuations — precisely because that is where retail positioning is most concentrated and most exploitable.

## Common SMC Mistakes

**1. Marking every candle as an order block.** Valid order blocks require structural significance — the move away must break a meaningful swing point. Arbitrary labeling produces noise, not signal.

**2. Trading order blocks without higher-timeframe confluence.** An order block on a 5-minute chart inside a strong higher-timeframe downtrend is a low-probability long setup regardless of how clean the pattern looks.

**3. Ignoring the session context.** An otherwise valid setup forming at 02:00 PM EST (NY lunch) carries far less weight than the same setup forming at 08:30 EST during the NY open killzone.

**4. Treating FVGs as guaranteed fills.** Not every FVG fills. Strong trending markets can leave multiple unfilled gaps. The presence of an FVG raises probability — it does not guarantee behavior.

**5. Confusing CHoCH with reversal confirmation.** A single CHoCH on a low timeframe is a warning, not a signal. Waiting for multi-timeframe structural alignment before committing capital is essential.

**6. Neglecting risk management.** SMC is a framework for identifying high-probability setups, not a certainty engine. Fixed risk per trade, proper stop placement below/above order blocks (not at arbitrary levels), and realistic reward:risk ratios remain non-negotiable.

**7. Overcomplicating the framework.** The core SMC edge is straightforward: identify where liquidity rests, anticipate the sweep, and enter in the post-sweep displacement at a structural mitigation zone. Every additional layer of complexity should serve that core logic — not replace it.

---

## Summary

Smart Money Concepts offers a coherent, institutional-centric framework for interpreting price action. Its core contribution is reorienting the retail trader's perspective — from reacting to price movements to anticipating why those movements occur based on where resting liquidity concentrates. When applied with proper multi-timeframe analysis, session awareness, and disciplined risk management, SMC provides a structured methodology for identifying high-probability entry points aligned with likely institutional activity. Its limitations are the limitations of all technical frameworks: markets are not perfectly predictable, and institutional behavior is inferred rather than directly observed.
