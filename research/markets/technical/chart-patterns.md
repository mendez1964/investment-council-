# Chart Patterns — Complete Reference

## Core Principle
Chart patterns are pictures of human psychology. Fear, greed, indecision, and conviction leave repeatable footprints in price charts. Understanding what each pattern means psychologically helps you trade them with conviction rather than memorizing shapes mechanically.

---

## Reversal Patterns — Trend Changes

### Head and Shoulders (Top) — Most Reliable Reversal Pattern

**What it looks like:**
- Left shoulder: Price rallies to a high, pulls back
- Head: Price rallies to a higher high (the head), pulls back to roughly the same level as before (the neckline)
- Right shoulder: Price rallies again but only to the level of the left shoulder (lower high), then pulls back

**The neckline:** Connect the two pullback lows (between left shoulder and head, and between head and right shoulder). This is the critical level.

**The entry:** When price breaks below the neckline on above-average volume after forming the right shoulder. This is the confirmation.

**The target:** Measure the distance from the head peak to the neckline. Subtract that distance from the neckline break point. This gives the measured move target.

**Why it works psychologically:** Three attempts to continue the uptrend — the third fails to reach the previous high. Each rally high is lower than the last. Buyers are getting exhausted. When the neckline breaks, it confirms the trend change and triggers stops from everyone who bought the pullbacks.

**Volume pattern:** Should see declining volume on the right shoulder rally and expanding volume on the neckline breakdown.

**False breakout warning:** Neckline breaks on low volume often reverse. Wait for volume confirmation.

### Head and Shoulders Bottom (Inverse Head and Shoulders)
Mirror image of the above, appearing after a downtrend. Left shoulder, lower head, right shoulder (higher low). Neckline connects the rally highs between the shoulders. Break above neckline on volume is the buy signal. Same measured move calculation applies (upward).

### Double Top
**Pattern:** Price reaches a high, pulls back 10-20%, rallies back to roughly the same high, fails to break through, and pulls back again.

**Psychology:** Buyers tried twice to push through resistance and failed. Supply overwhelmed demand at that level twice. This tells you: at that price, there are enough sellers to stop the advance.

**Entry:** Break below the pullback low (the trough between the two tops) on volume. This is the confirmation line.

**Target:** Measure the height from the double top high to the trough. Subtract from the breakdown point.

**Critical nuance:** The two tops do not need to be at the exact same price — within 2-3% is close enough. What matters is the failure to make a meaningful new high.

### Double Bottom
Mirror image. Two lows at roughly the same price level. Break above the peak between the two lows is the buy signal. Common at the end of bear markets and major corrections.

### Triple Top and Triple Bottom
Three tests of the same resistance (triple top) or support (triple bottom). Each additional test weakens the level — the more times it is tested without breaking, the more likely the eventual break will be explosive. Entry and target same as double top/bottom.

### Rounding Bottom (Saucer)
**Pattern:** A slow, gradual arc downward followed by a slow, gradual arc upward — forming a bowl or saucer shape. Often takes months to form.

**Psychology:** Reflects a slow transition from distribution (selling) to accumulation (buying). No dramatic events — just a gradual shift in the balance of supply and demand.

**Entry:** When price breaks above the rim of the saucer on volume — the right side of the bowl matches the height of the left side.

**Reliability:** High — when these patterns break out, they tend to trend well because they reflect genuine accumulation, not just short-term momentum.

### Rounding Top (Inverse Saucer)
Mirror image — a slow topping process. Less common than rounding bottom.

### Rising Wedge
**Pattern:** Price makes higher highs and higher lows but the range is narrowing — both the highs and lows are converging upward. Looks like price is being squeezed upward.

**Psychology:** Each rally is weaker than the last. Buyers can still push price higher but with less and less conviction. This is distribution — smart money selling into the rallies.

**Signal:** Bearish reversal. Break below the lower trendline on volume is the entry trigger for a short.

**Context matters:** Rising wedge after an extended uptrend = high-conviction reversal signal. Rising wedge at the beginning of a move = less reliable.

### Falling Wedge
**Pattern:** Price makes lower lows and lower highs but range is narrowing downward.

**Psychology:** Each decline is losing momentum. Sellers can push price lower but with less force. Accumulation occurring.

**Signal:** Bullish reversal. Break above the upper trendline is the entry trigger. One of the more reliable bullish patterns.

## Continuation Patterns — Trend Resumes

### Bull Flag
**Pattern:** After a strong, nearly vertical price advance (the flagpole), price consolidates in a tight downward-sloping channel (the flag) on declining volume.

**Psychology:** The initial surge attracts aggressive buyers. Profit-takers slow the advance. But there is not enough selling pressure to drive the stock back — sellers are weak. When buyers re-enter, the advance continues.

**Entry:** Break above the upper trendline of the flag channel on volume
**Stop loss:** Below the lower trendline of the flag channel
**Target:** Add the flagpole length to the breakout point

**Volume signature:** High volume on the flagpole, declining volume during the flag formation, expanding volume on the breakout. This volume pattern is the confirmation.

**Best context:** Bull flags work best when the flagpole was strong (5-15% move), the flag is tight (not more than 50% retracement of the pole), and the consolidation is 5-15 trading days.

### Bear Flag
Mirror image. Sharp decline (pole), tight upward-sloping consolidation (flag) on declining volume, then breakdown on volume.

### Bull Pennant
Similar to bull flag but the consolidation forms symmetrical converging trendlines (a triangle) rather than a channel. Same entry, stop, and target calculation.

### Bear Pennant
Mirror image of bull pennant.

### Cup and Handle
**Pattern:** A rounded bottom (the cup) followed by a small, tight downward correction (the handle). The handle should not retrace more than 50% of the cup's advance.

**Psychology:** The cup represents a period of basing and accumulation. The handle shakes out weak holders before the real move. The handle on light volume confirms that selling pressure is minimal.

**Entry:** Break above the right rim of the cup (at the level of the left rim) after the handle formation. This is the pivot point.
**Stop loss:** Below the low of the handle
**Target:** Add the depth of the cup to the breakout point

**The ideal cup:** U-shaped (not V-shaped), 7-65 weeks long, no more than 30-35% deep in normal markets.

**William O'Neil** developed this pattern extensively. He said most of the greatest stock market winners formed a cup and handle at some point in their advance.

### Ascending Triangle
**Pattern:** A flat resistance level at the top (seller keeps stopping the advance at the same price) and higher lows at the bottom (buyers getting more aggressive each time).

**Psychology:** Buyers are willing to pay more and more each time. But a specific price level has strong supply (a seller is waiting). Eventually demand exceeds the supply at that level and price breaks through.

**Entry:** Break above the flat resistance line on volume
**Stop loss:** Below the most recent higher low
**Target:** Measure the height of the widest part of the triangle, add to the breakout point

### Descending Triangle
Mirror image. Flat support at the bottom (buyer absorbing at same level), lower highs (sellers getting more aggressive). Break below the flat support is the entry for a short.

### Symmetrical Triangle
Neither buyers nor sellers gaining ground — converging highs and lows. A consolidation pattern with no inherent directional bias. Typically breaks in the direction of the prior trend.

**Entry:** Break above upper or below lower trendline on volume — trade in the direction of the break
**Target:** Same measured move calculation

### Rectangle Consolidation
Price moves sideways between two roughly parallel horizontal levels — a defined range. Continuation pattern.

**Entry:** Break above the top of the rectangle (continuation of prior uptrend) or below the bottom (continuation of prior downtrend) on volume
**Target:** Add the height of the rectangle to the breakout point

## Measured Move Calculation
Every pattern has a measurable target:
1. Measure the height of the pattern (from the most extreme point to the breakout level)
2. Add (for bullish patterns) or subtract (for bearish patterns) that distance from the breakout point
3. This gives the minimum measured move target

Example: Head and shoulders with head at $100 and neckline at $80. Height = $20. Target after neckline break = $80 - $20 = $60.

## Common False Breakout Traps

**The stop run:** Market makers and institutional algorithms know where retail stops are clustered. They will often push price just above a resistance level (triggering buy orders) or just below a support level (triggering sell stops) and then reverse.

**How to avoid:** Wait for a close beyond the level, not just an intraday poke. A candle that closes beyond the pattern boundary is stronger than one that merely touches it intraday.

**The volume tell:** A breakout on below-average volume is much more likely to fail. Wait for volume to confirm.

**The retest opportunity:** Most genuine breakouts will retest the breakout level from above (for upside breaks) before continuing. This retest gives a second, often lower-risk entry.
