# Momentum Indicators — Complete Reference

## What Momentum Indicators Do
Momentum indicators measure the speed and strength of price movement. They tell you whether buying or selling pressure is increasing or decreasing — separate from the price direction itself. Their greatest value is in divergence — when the indicator tells a different story than the price chart.

---

## RSI — Relative Strength Index

### What It Is
Developed by J. Welles Wilder. Measures the magnitude of recent gains versus recent losses to evaluate overbought or oversold conditions. Scale: 0 to 100.

### Standard Settings
**14-period RSI** is the default. Use 14 on any timeframe (14 days for daily charts, 14 hours for hourly, etc.)

### Basic Interpretation
- **Above 70:** Overbought — the stock has risen quickly and may be due for a pullback
- **Below 30:** Oversold — the stock has fallen quickly and may be due for a bounce
- **50 level:** The bull/bear dividing line. RSI above 50 = bullish momentum. Below 50 = bearish momentum.

**Critical warning:** Overbought does not mean sell. In a strong trend, RSI can stay above 70 for extended periods. A stock that is consistently "overbought" by RSI is showing strong momentum — not necessarily a reason to sell.

### RSI Divergence — The Most Powerful Signal

**Bullish divergence:** Price makes a lower low but RSI makes a higher low. The price is going lower but the selling pressure is weakening — momentum is diverging from price. This is an early warning of a potential trend reversal upward.

Example: Stock drops from $100 to $80, then bounces to $90, then falls to $75 (lower low). But RSI at the $75 low is higher than RSI was at the $80 low. Price is weaker but momentum is stronger. This divergence often precedes a significant rally.

**Bearish divergence:** Price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high. The price is going higher but the buying pressure is weakening. Early warning of potential reversal downward.

Example: Stock rallies to $100, pulls back, rallies to $105 (higher high). RSI at $105 is lower than RSI was at $100. The upward momentum is fading even as price makes new highs. Distribution occurring.

**Hidden bullish divergence:** Price makes a higher low (healthy correction) but RSI makes a lower low. Signals continuation of the uptrend — the pullback is normal, the underlying trend is intact.

**Hidden bearish divergence:** Price makes a lower high (weak bounce) but RSI makes a higher high. Signals continuation of the downtrend.

### RSI in Strong Trends
In a powerful uptrend, RSI stays in the 50-80 range and rarely drops below 40. The 40 level becomes support for RSI during pullbacks. This is called "bullish RSI range."

In a powerful downtrend, RSI stays in the 20-50 range and rarely rallies above 60. The 60 level becomes resistance. "Bearish RSI range."

When RSI breaks its trend range, it signals a potential trend change.

### RSI Failure Swings
A signal that does not require price comparison:

**Bullish failure swing:** RSI drops below 30 (oversold), bounces above 30, pulls back but holds above 30, then breaks above the recent bounce high. This break is the signal — not dependent on price at all.

**Bearish failure swing:** RSI rises above 70 (overbought), falls below 70, bounces but cannot get back above 70, then falls below the recent bounce low. This break is the signal.

### Multi-Timeframe RSI
RSI on the weekly chart is more significant than RSI on the daily. Before entering based on daily RSI signals, check the weekly RSI to make sure you are not fighting a larger trend.

Weekly RSI oversold in an otherwise healthy stock = high conviction long opportunity
Weekly RSI overbought in a deteriorating business = high conviction short consideration

## MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence

### What It Is
Developed by Gerald Appel. Shows the relationship between two exponential moving averages. The standard settings: 12-period EMA minus 26-period EMA = MACD line. A 9-period EMA of the MACD line = Signal line. The difference between MACD line and signal line = Histogram.

### Standard Settings: 12, 26, 9

### The Three MACD Signals

**1. MACD Line/Signal Line Crossover:**
- MACD crosses above signal line = bullish momentum — buy signal
- MACD crosses below signal line = bearish momentum — sell signal
- Most reliable in trending markets, unreliable in choppy markets

**2. The Zero Line:**
- MACD crosses above zero = the 12 EMA has crossed above the 26 EMA — trend has turned bullish
- MACD crosses below zero = trend has turned bearish
- Zero line crossovers are slower than signal crossovers but more reliable

**3. MACD Histogram:**
- Growing histogram bars (in either direction) = momentum increasing
- Shrinking histogram bars = momentum decreasing — early warning of potential crossover
- The histogram shrinking before a crossover gives advance notice of the signal

### MACD Divergence
Same principle as RSI divergence but using the MACD line.

**Bullish divergence:** Price makes lower lows but MACD makes higher lows. Selling momentum is decreasing even as price falls. Potential reversal signal.

**Bearish divergence:** Price makes higher highs but MACD makes lower highs. Buying momentum is decreasing even as price rises. Potential reversal signal.

### MACD in Trending vs Ranging Markets
In trending markets, MACD is an excellent trend-following tool — the crossovers keep you on the right side of the trend.

In ranging markets, MACD generates endless false crossovers. In a sideways market, MACD is nearly worthless. This is its primary limitation.

**Solution:** Combine MACD with ADX (Average Directional Index). If ADX is below 20, the market is ranging — ignore MACD signals. If ADX is above 25, the market is trending — use MACD.

## Stochastics

### What It Is
Developed by George Lane. Compares a security's closing price to its price range over a specific period. Scale: 0 to 100.

**Formula:** %K = (Close - Lowest Low over period) / (Highest High - Lowest Low over period) × 100

### Fast vs Slow Stochastics
**Fast Stochastics:** More sensitive, more signals, more noise. Standard: 14,3,3
**Slow Stochastics:** Smoothed version, fewer signals, more reliable. This is what most traders use.

### Basic Interpretation
- **Above 80:** Overbought
- **Below 20:** Oversold
- **%K crossing above %D:** Bullish signal
- **%K crossing below %D:** Bearish signal

### When Stochastics Work Best
Stochastics perform best in **ranging, oscillating markets** — the opposite of MACD. When price is bouncing between support and resistance, stochastics reliably identify turning points.

**Stochastics trading the range:**
- Buy when stochastics reach oversold (below 20) at support
- Sell when stochastics reach overbought (above 80) at resistance
- Works repeatedly until the range breaks

### When Stochastics Fail
In strong trending markets, stochastics can stay in extreme territory indefinitely. A strong uptrend will keep stochastics above 80 for weeks. Selling every time it is "overbought" will generate loss after loss.

**The solution:** In trending markets, use only the stochastics signals that align with the trend direction. In an uptrend, only buy the oversold signals — ignore the overbought sell signals.

### Stochastics Divergence
Same principle as RSI and MACD. Price makes a new low but stochastics makes a higher low = bullish divergence. More reliable when it occurs in the oversold zone.

## Combining Momentum Indicators

**Never use the same type of indicator twice.** RSI and Stochastics are both oscillators measuring similar things — using both gives you no additional information.

**The power combination:**
1. MACD for trend identification (is momentum trending?)
2. RSI for overbought/oversold and divergence
3. Volume for confirmation

**The divergence confirmation rule:** The highest-conviction trade setup is when both RSI and MACD show divergence at the same time. When two separate indicators are telling you the same story about fading momentum, the probability of a reversal is significantly higher.

## Momentum Indicators Applied to Different Markets

**Stocks:** RSI and MACD work very well on daily and weekly charts. Look for divergences on earnings pullbacks and sector corrections.

**Crypto:** RSI is particularly useful because crypto moves in extreme momentum waves. RSI above 85 in Bitcoin often precedes major corrections. RSI below 25 often marks generational buying opportunities.

**Options:** Use momentum indicators on the underlying stock to time options entry and exit. IV (implied volatility) is a separate consideration.

**Futures:** MACD works well for identifying trend entry points on daily charts. Stochastics for timing entries within swing setups.
