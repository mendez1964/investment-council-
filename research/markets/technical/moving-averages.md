# Moving Averages — Complete Reference

## What Moving Averages Are
A moving average smooths price data by calculating the average closing price over a specific number of periods. It filters out noise and shows the underlying trend direction.

---

## SMA vs EMA

**Simple Moving Average (SMA):** All periods weighted equally. Smoother, slower to respond to price changes. Better for identifying major trends and key support/resistance levels. Less prone to whipsaws.

**Exponential Moving Average (EMA):** Recent periods weighted more heavily. Responds faster to price changes. Better for shorter-term trend following and momentum trading. More prone to whipsaws in choppy markets.

**Rule of thumb:** Use EMA for active trading and shorter timeframes. Use SMA for major trend identification and longer timeframes.

## The Key Moving Averages

### 9 EMA — Short-term Momentum
- **Purpose:** Short-term momentum indicator. The fastest of the common moving averages.
- **Who uses it:** Day traders and scalpers.
- **What it tells you:** Whether price has immediate momentum. Stocks in strong momentum phases stay above the 9 EMA continuously.
- **How to use it:** In a strong uptrend, buy pullbacks to the 9 EMA. When price breaks below and cannot reclaim the 9 EMA, momentum has shifted.
- **Warning:** Too fast for swing trading — creates whipsaws in any normal consolidation.

### 20 EMA — Swing Trading Trend
- **Purpose:** The key moving average for swing traders. Defines the near-term trend.
- **Who uses it:** Swing traders primarily.
- **What it tells you:** Whether the intermediate trend is bullish or bearish. Healthy bull markets pull back to the 20 EMA and bounce.
- **The setup:** Stock in an uptrend pulls back to 20 EMA on declining volume, finds support, and begins to turn up — this is one of Lynch's favorite setups and a core swing trading entry.
- **The break:** When price closes clearly below the 20 EMA and the 20 EMA begins to flatten or turn down, the intermediate trend has changed.

### 50 SMA — Intermediate Trend
- **Purpose:** The dividing line between healthy uptrend (price above 50 SMA) and correcting stock (price below 50 SMA).
- **Who uses it:** Swing traders and position traders. Widely watched by institutions.
- **What it tells you:** The intermediate trend health of a stock. Major test during corrections.
- **The setup:** Stock corrects from highs, finds support exactly at the 50 SMA, bounces. This is a textbook swing long setup if fundamentals are intact.
- **The break:** A stock that breaks below its rising 50 SMA is making a statement. Either the company is in trouble or the overall market has turned. Requires reassessment.

### 100 SMA — Medium-term Trend
- **Purpose:** A less commonly cited but useful intermediate level between 50 and 200.
- **What it tells you:** If a stock breaks the 50 SMA but holds the 100 SMA, the correction may still be healthy in a bull market.
- **Use case:** In a strong bull market, the 100 SMA often acts as the deepest support level before the 200 SMA.

### 200 SMA — The Bull/Bear Line
- **Purpose:** The most widely followed moving average. Separates long-term bull markets from bear markets.
- **Who uses it:** Everyone — from retail traders to institutional portfolio managers.
- **What it tells you:** The long-term trend. Above 200 SMA = stock in long-term uptrend. Below = long-term downtrend.
- **Paul Tudor Jones rule:** He will not be long a stock that is below its 200-day moving average. He considers it too risky.
- **Market breadth signal:** The percentage of S&P 500 stocks above their 200 SMA is one of the most reliable market health indicators. Above 70% = healthy bull market. Below 30% = deeply oversold, potential bottom. Falling rapidly from above 70% toward 50% = early warning.

## The Golden Cross and Death Cross

**Golden Cross:** The 50 SMA crosses above the 200 SMA. One of the most widely watched technical signals.
- Signals: Long-term trend has shifted to bullish
- Occurs: After a bear market or prolonged correction when price recovers
- Important caveat: It is a lagging indicator — it confirms a trend change that has already happened. Often occurs after much of the early move is already done. Use it for confirmation, not as an early entry.

**Death Cross:** The 50 SMA crosses below the 200 SMA. The bearish equivalent.
- Signals: Long-term trend has shifted to bearish
- Same caveat: Lagging. Often triggers after much of the initial decline has already occurred.
- The market sometimes rally after a death cross forms — it is a warning, not a timed short signal.

## Moving Average Stacks — Bullish and Bearish Alignment

**Fully Bullish Stack (9/20/50/200 all aligned):**
- 9 EMA > 20 EMA > 50 SMA > 200 SMA
- Price above all four
- All moving averages pointing upward
This is the textbook setup for position trading long. Every timeframe is aligned bullish.

**Fully Bearish Stack:**
- 9 EMA < 20 EMA < 50 SMA < 200 SMA
- Price below all four
- All moving averages pointing downward
This is the setup for considering short sales or avoiding long positions.

**The mixed stack:** When 9 EMA > 20 EMA but both are below 50 SMA — short-term bounce in a longer-term downtrend. Be cautious with longs.

## Moving Average as Dynamic Support and Resistance

Moving averages act as support in uptrends and resistance in downtrends.

**Uptrend test of moving average:**
In a healthy uptrend, pullbacks often find support at rising moving averages. The 20 EMA is the first test. If that breaks, the 50 SMA is the next. If that breaks, the 200 SMA is the major test.

**Trading the test:**
- Stock pulls back to rising 20 EMA on declining volume
- Volume dries up at the 20 EMA — sellers exhausted
- A hammer or bullish engulfing candle forms at the 20 EMA
- Next day opens above the 20 EMA with volume returning
- Entry: Break above the reversal candle's high
- Stop: Below the 20 EMA (a few percent)
This is one of the cleanest setups in all of swing trading.

**Downtrend bounce to moving average:**
In a downtrend, bounces often fail at declining moving averages. A stock below its 50 SMA will often rally to the 50 SMA and then fail. Short selling opportunity.

## The Moving Average Ribbon

Place multiple moving averages on one chart — for example the 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, and 60 EMAs together.

**When the ribbon is spread apart and pointing upward:** Strong trend — trend followers are in profit across all timeframes.

**When the ribbon converges:** Trend is slowing — momentum is fading, consolidation likely.

**When the ribbon reversal:** Short-term MAs crossing through longer-term MAs from top to bottom signals beginning of new downtrend.

## VWAP — Volume Weighted Moving Average

VWAP is not a traditional moving average but deserves mention here as the most important intraday dynamic level.

**Calculation:** Sum of (price × volume) divided by total volume for the day. Resets daily.

**Key VWAP trading rules:**
1. Open above VWAP with bullish candle — look for long setups
2. Open below VWAP with bearish candle — look for short setups
3. First pullback to VWAP after strong open often provides the best trade of the day
4. Stock holding above VWAP all day = institutional buying support
5. Stock failing at VWAP repeatedly = institutional selling at that level

**VWAP standard deviation bands:**
Commonly plotted at ±1, ±2, and ±3 standard deviations from VWAP.
Price at +2 SD is extended — mean reversion likely
Price at -2 SD is oversold intraday — bounce possible
Price at ±3 SD is extreme — high probability of at least partial reversion

## When Moving Averages Stop Working

Moving averages work best in trending markets. In choppy, sideways markets they are almost useless — they will generate false signals repeatedly.

**Signs you are in a choppy market:**
- Price keeps crossing above and below the moving average
- The moving average itself is flat (horizontal)
- Each signal from the MA reverses quickly
- No sustained momentum after MA crossovers

**The solution:** When price action becomes choppy and MAs are giving false signals, reduce position size or stop trading MA-based setups entirely until trend resumes. Switch to mean-reversion strategies or simply wait.
