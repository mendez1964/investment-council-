## Gold

### Core Price Drivers

Gold does not produce cash flows, so its price is determined almost entirely by opportunity cost and sentiment. The dominant quantitative driver is **real interest rates** — specifically the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield minus the 10-year breakeven inflation rate (the TIPS-derived real yield). When real rates fall or go negative, the cost of holding zero-yield gold drops, and the metal rallies. When real rates rise sharply, as in 2022, gold faces sustained headwinds even with elevated inflation. This inverse relationship is tighter than almost any other macro variable and should be the first chart traders check.

Secondary drivers include:

- **Dollar strength** — Gold is priced in USD globally; a weaker dollar makes gold cheaper in other currencies, expanding demand and lifting the price.
- **Central bank purchases** — Emerging market central banks (China, India, Poland, Turkey) have been net buyers for over a decade, providing structural demand.
- **Geopolitical risk premiums** — War, sanctions, and financial system stress trigger safe-haven flows that can override the real-rates signal for weeks or months.
- **ETF flows** — GLD and IAU aggregate Western investment demand into a visible, daily-published figure. Rising ETF holdings during a rally confirm institutional conviction; falling holdings during a rally is a warning sign.

### Safe Haven Mechanics

Gold's safe-haven properties activate under specific stress conditions: systemic financial panic (2008, March 2020), currency crises, and sovereign debt anxiety. Notably, gold can sell off sharply during acute liquidity crises as leveraged funds raise cash — the March 2020 dip being the textbook example — before recovering violently once central banks respond. Traders should distinguish between a **liquidity-driven selloff** (temporary, buy the dip) and a **rate-driven selloff** (structural, wait for real yields to stabilize).

### GLD ETF and Physical Access

The SPDR Gold Shares (GLD) is the most liquid gold ETF with over $60B in AUM. Each share represents approximately 0.0927 troy ounces. The iShares Gold Trust (IAU) charges a lower expense ratio (0.25% vs 0.40%) and is preferred for long-term holders. Neither ETF provides exposure to mining economics — they track spot gold directly.

Physical gold (coins, bars) carries premiums of 3–8% over spot and storage/insurance costs, but has zero counterparty risk — a consideration during extreme financial stress scenarios.

### Miners vs Physical

Gold miners offer leveraged exposure: a 10% move in gold might produce a 20–30% move in a senior miner due to operating leverage (fixed costs magnify margin expansion). The VanEck Gold Miners ETF (GDX) tracks senior miners; GDXJ covers juniors with higher risk and higher potential upside. Key metrics for miner analysis: **all-in sustaining cost (AISC)**, free cash flow yield, hedge book exposure, and jurisdiction risk. Miners also introduce management risk and capital allocation risk absent in physical.

Royalty companies (Franco-Nevada, Royal Gold, Wheaton Precious Metals) offer a third path — gold price leverage with lower operational risk, since they fund mine development in exchange for a percentage of production rather than operating mines themselves.

### Gold/Silver Ratio

The ratio of gold price to silver price (currently ranging 80–90x historically) signals relative value between the metals. A ratio above 80 historically favors silver outperformance in subsequent months; a ratio below 50 suggests gold is relatively cheap. The ratio compresses during risk-on commodity cycles as silver's industrial demand adds a kicker, and expands during risk-off periods when gold's safe-haven premium dominates.

## Silver

### Dual Demand Structure

Silver sits at the intersection of precious metals and industrial commodities — roughly 50–55% of demand is industrial, 30% is investment/jewelry, and the balance is silverware. This split makes silver more volatile than gold and gives it a distinct macro profile.

### Industrial and Solar Demand

Silver is a critical input in solar photovoltaic panels (each panel uses approximately 20 grams of silver for conductive paste). The global build-out of solar capacity has become a structural demand driver with no near-term substitute at scale. Additional industrial uses include electronics, EV charging infrastructure, 5G components, and medical devices. These demand streams make silver sensitive to global manufacturing PMIs and technology capex cycles in ways that gold is not.

### Volatility and Trading Characteristics

Silver is approximately 2–3x more volatile than gold on a percentage basis. It tends to lag gold at the start of a precious metals rally, then accelerate sharply — a pattern known as "silver catching up to gold." The SLV and PSLV ETFs are the primary vehicles for paper silver exposure. The futures market (COMEX) is highly speculative, with significant managed money positioning that can drive sharp short-covering rallies.

## Oil

### WTI vs Brent Spread

West Texas Intermediate (WTI) and Brent crude are the two global benchmarks. Brent is the international standard, priced in the North Sea, and serves as the reference for most global contracts. WTI is the U.S. benchmark, delivered at Cushing, Oklahoma. The **Brent-WTI spread** typically runs $2–5/barrel with Brent at a premium, reflecting transportation and quality differences. The spread widens when U.S. storage at Cushing fills (increasing WTI discount) and compresses when export infrastructure expands.

### OPEC+ Mechanics

OPEC+ (OPEC members plus Russia and allies) controls approximately 40% of global oil production. The cartel sets production targets at ministerial meetings; compliance varies by member, and Iraq and Russia have historically been serial over-producers relative to quota. Markets price OPEC+ decisions primarily on:

1. **Quota changes** — headline additions or cuts to production targets
2. **Compliance rates** — actual production vs target (tracked via tanker data and secondary surveys)
3. **Spare capacity** — Saudi Arabia holds most of the world's swing capacity; when it's low, supply shocks are harder to absorb

### Inventory Data

The two key weekly U.S. inventory reports are the **API report** (released Tuesday afternoon) and the **EIA report** (released Wednesday morning). A crude draw (inventory decline) is bullish; a build is bearish. Traders focus on:
- Crude at Cushing (WTI-specific)
- Gasoline inventories (demand signal)
- Distillate inventories (diesel demand — more economically sensitive)

### Crack Spread

The crack spread is the difference between crude oil cost and the value of refined products — the refiner's gross margin. The **3-2-1 crack spread** (3 barrels of crude producing 2 barrels of gasoline + 1 barrel of distillate) is the standard measure. Rising crack spreads signal strong product demand and support integrated oil majors and pure-play refiners. The Valero (VLO) and Phillips 66 (PSX) are key refiners to watch.

### Oil Sector Structure

- **Integrated Majors** (ExxonMobil, Chevron, Shell, BP): upstream + downstream + chemicals; more diversified, pay dividends, less volatile
- **E&P (Exploration & Production)** (Pioneer, Devon, Diamondback, Coterra): pure upstream leverage to oil/gas prices; higher beta, free cash flow yields are key metric
- **Oil Services** (Schlumberger/SLB, Halliburton, Baker Hughes): leveraged to drilling activity rather than commodity price directly; tend to lag E&P in cycle recoveries

## Copper

### Dr. Copper as Economic Indicator

Copper's nickname "Dr. Copper" reflects its use in virtually every sector of a modern economy — construction, wiring, plumbing, electronics, and transportation. Because of this breadth, copper price trends have historically provided reliable early signals about global economic acceleration or deceleration, often leading equity markets. A sustained copper rally with rising LME inventories is a different signal than a rally with falling inventories.

### China Demand

China consumes approximately 55% of global refined copper, making Chinese economic data — particularly fixed asset investment, property starts, and manufacturing PMI — the dominant short-term demand driver. The periodic release of Chinese copper import data and SHFE (Shanghai Futures Exchange) warehouse inventory levels are critical data points. Property sector stress in China is a persistent headwind for copper demand and should be weighed against electrification tailwinds.

### Supply Constraints and Electrification

Long lead times (10–15 years from discovery to production), permitting challenges, and declining ore grades at major mines (Escondida in Chile, Grasberg in Indonesia) create structural supply constraints. Electrification of transportation (each EV uses 4x more copper than an ICE vehicle), grid build-out for renewables, and data center expansion (AI-driven power demand) represent multi-decade demand growth. This supply-demand imbalance is the core thesis for the copper bull case. FCX (Freeport-McMoRan) is the primary pure-play U.S.-listed copper miner.

## Agricultural Commodities

### Key Crops: Corn, Wheat, Soybeans

The **grain complex** is driven by a different set of inputs than metals or energy:

- **Weather** is the dominant short-term driver — drought in the U.S. Corn Belt (June–August), the Black Sea region, or South American growing areas (December–February for soybeans) can cause significant price spikes. Traders monitor the NOAA drought monitor and seasonal El Nino/La Nina forecasts.
- **USDA Reports** — The monthly WASDE (World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates) report is the most market-moving release in agricultural commodities, providing updated supply, demand, and ending stocks estimates. The annual March Prospective Plantings and June 1 Stocks reports are also high-impact.
- **Geopolitics** — Russia and Ukraine together supply approximately 30% of global wheat exports and a significant share of corn exports. The 2022 invasion caused a major supply shock. Black Sea shipping conditions and export tariff policies remain ongoing price variables.
- **Currency effects** — A strong Brazilian real raises Brazilian soybean export costs in dollar terms, improving U.S. competitiveness. The USD/BRL and USD/ARS (Argentina) rates matter significantly.

Corn, wheat, and soybeans trade on CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange). WEAT, CORN, and SOYB are ETF proxies, though subject to roll costs.

## Commodity Supercycles

### Historical Context

Commodity supercycles are extended periods (15–20+ years) of broadly rising real commodity prices, driven by structural demand shifts that outpace the supply response. The four recognized modern supercycles:
1. **Late 1800s**: U.S. industrialization and railroad buildout
2. **WWII era**: wartime industrial demand
3. **1970s**: oil shocks, dollar devaluation, inflationary spiral
4. **2000s China cycle**: China's entry into the WTO and infrastructure buildout drove a decade-long commodity surge (2000–2008 peak, with a 2009 reset and second leg to 2011)

### What Drives Supercycles

Three conditions tend to coincide: (1) a major new demand source (industrialization of a large economy, electrification, war mobilization), (2) a period of underinvestment in supply that creates a multi-year lag in the production response, and (3) a structural dollar weakening cycle that inflates commodity prices in USD terms.

### Current Cycle

The current potential supercycle thesis centers on **green energy transition** demand — copper, lithium, cobalt, nickel, and silver all required in quantity — coinciding with years of underinvestment in mining capital following the 2015–2016 commodity bust. The counter-argument: China's slowing growth and the slower-than-expected EV adoption curve are tempering the demand side. The debate is active; position sizing and stop discipline matter more in a contested cycle than a confirmed one.

## Contango vs Backwardation

### Mechanics

Futures markets for commodities exist in either **contango** (later-dated futures priced higher than spot) or **backwardation** (later-dated futures priced lower than spot).

- **Contango** reflects storage costs and the cost of carry. It is the normal condition for storable commodities when supply is ample. Oil was in extreme contango in April 2020 when WTI front-month futures briefly went negative.
- **Backwardation** occurs when immediate physical supply is tight relative to deferred supply. It signals that the market values spot delivery at a premium — a bullish structural signal. Oil markets frequently enter backwardation during supply-constrained periods.

### Impact on ETF Holders

This is a critical and often misunderstood risk for commodity ETF investors. Most commodity ETFs (USO for oil, UNG for natural gas) hold front-month futures and must **roll** them forward as expiration approaches. In contango, the ETF sells expiring contracts at a lower price and buys the next month at a higher price — a perpetual drag called **negative roll yield**. Over time, this causes commodity ETFs to dramatically underperform spot prices. USO lost the majority of its value over multi-year periods even when oil prices were flat-to-up.

In backwardation, rolling futures generates **positive roll yield** — the ETF benefits from selling expiring contracts at a premium. This is why commodity markets in backwardation can be particularly attractive for ETF holders.

The solution: use commodity producer equities for long-duration exposure, or use ETFs that hold longer-dated futures curves (e.g., DJP, GSG) which reduce but do not eliminate roll drag.

## How to Trade Commodities

### Instruments and Their Tradeoffs

| Instrument | Leverage | Counterparty Risk | Roll Drag | Complexity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Futures contracts | High | Exchange-cleared | N/A (you manage it) | High |
| Commodity ETFs | 1x (unless leveraged) | Low | Significant in contango | Low |
| Commodity producer stocks | Implicit operating leverage | Company risk | None | Medium |
| Royalty/streaming companies | Moderate leverage | Low | None | Medium |
| Physical (gold/silver) | 1x | None | None | Low |

**Futures** offer the purest commodity exposure and are appropriate for active traders with margin experience. Position limits, margin calls, and delivery mechanics require active management.

**ETFs** are accessible but roll drag makes them unsuitable for long-duration holds in contango markets. Best used for short-term tactical trades.

**Producer equities** are the preferred vehicle for long-duration commodity exposure for most investors — operating leverage to commodity prices with no roll drag, plus dividends and buybacks in mature cycles.

**Royalty companies** (Franco-Nevada, Royal Gold, Wheaton, Sandstorm, Triple Flag) provide commodity exposure with lower capital intensity, better margins, and diversification across many mines and metals. Premium valuations reflect these advantages.

## Dollar Correlation

The U.S. dollar index (DXY) maintains a generally inverse relationship with commodity prices. Because most commodities are priced in dollars globally, dollar strength makes them more expensive in foreign currency terms, reducing demand and compressing prices. The correlation is strongest and most consistent with gold, oil, and the broad commodity complex. It is looser for agricultural commodities, where local supply/demand conditions often dominate.

Dollar weakness driven by Fed dovishness or fiscal deterioration is historically among the most reliable setups for broad commodity bull runs. The 2001–2008 commodity supercycle coincided with a 40% DXY decline.

## Inflation Hedge Properties

Commodities are direct inflation hedges because they are the inputs that drive consumer prices — food, energy, and raw materials constitute the majority of CPI. When inflation is rising due to supply shocks (energy, food), commodities both cause and benefit from the inflation. When inflation is demand-driven, commodity prices often rise in tandem with broader economic growth.

However, commodities are **volatile and cyclical** inflation hedges, not smooth or reliable in short windows. A diversified real assets approach — combining commodity exposure, TIPS, real estate, and commodity equities — provides more consistent inflation protection than pure commodity exposure alone.
