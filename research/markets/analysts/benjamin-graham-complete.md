# Benjamin Graham — Complete Analyst Framework

## Who He Was
Benjamin Graham (1894-1976) is the father of value investing and fundamental securities analysis. His books Security Analysis (1934, co-authored with David Dodd) and The Intelligent Investor (1949) defined modern investment analysis. He taught at Columbia Business School where Warren Buffett was his student. He called his approach a systematic, disciplined method for determining what a security is actually worth — independent of what the market says it is worth on any given day.

---

## The Core Insight: Mr. Market

Graham's most enduring contribution is the allegory of Mr. Market. Imagine you own a share of a business alongside a partner called Mr. Market. Every day, Mr. Market offers to buy your share or sell you his at a specific price. The price varies wildly — sometimes absurdly high, sometimes absurdly low — based on his emotional state.

**The key:** Mr. Market's daily price is an offer, not a valuation. You do not have to accept it. You can wait. You can buy from him when he is depressed and selling cheap. You can sell to him when he is euphoric and paying too much. The intelligent investor takes advantage of Mr. Market's irrationality rather than being influenced by it.

---

## The Margin of Safety — The Central Concept

**Definition:** The difference between the intrinsic value you calculate and the price you pay. If a stock is worth $100 in your analysis and you pay $70, your margin of safety is 30%.

**Why it matters:**
- Your analysis may be wrong — the margin of safety protects against errors
- External events may harm the business — the margin of safety absorbs unexpected damage
- The market may stay irrational longer than expected — the margin of safety allows you to hold

**Graham's rule:** Never pay full price. Seek at minimum a 33% margin of safety on any investment.

## Intrinsic Value Methods

### Net Net Working Capital
The most conservative valuation method. If you can buy a business for less than its working capital minus ALL liabilities, you are essentially getting the business for free.

**Formula:**
Current Assets − Total Liabilities = Net Net Working Capital
Net Net Working Capital ÷ Shares Outstanding = Net Net per Share

If the stock price is below the Net Net per Share: Potentially deep value.

**Context:** Graham called these "cigar butt" investments — even a dying business may have one last puff of value if you can buy it for less than its liquidation value.

**In today's market:** True net-nets are rare in the US market. They exist more commonly in international markets, particularly Japan and smaller emerging markets.

### The Graham Number
A simplified intrinsic value estimate combining earnings and book value.

**Formula:** Graham Number = √(22.5 × EPS × Book Value Per Share)

**What 22.5 means:** Graham believed a PE ratio above 15 was too expensive and a Price-to-Book ratio above 1.5 was too expensive. 15 × 1.5 = 22.5.

**Interpretation:** If the stock trades below the Graham Number, it may be undervalued by Graham's standards.

**Example:** Stock with EPS of $3.00 and book value of $20 per share.
Graham Number = √(22.5 × $3.00 × $20) = √$1,350 = $36.74
If the stock trades at $30, it is below the Graham Number.

### Earnings Power Value (EPV)
Assumes the business earns its current (normalized) earnings forever with zero growth.

**Formula:** EPV = Normalized EBIT × (1 - Tax Rate) ÷ Cost of Capital

**Why it is useful:** It strips out growth assumptions entirely. If EPV exceeds the current stock price, you are being paid for the earnings even if the business never grows.

## The Defensive Investor Criteria (Seven Requirements)

Graham defined criteria for a "defensive investor" — someone who wants safety and minimal effort. All seven criteria must be met.

**1. Adequate Size**
Minimum annual sales of $100 million for industrial companies, $50 million for utilities. Small companies are too vulnerable.

**2. Strong Financial Condition**
Current ratio (current assets ÷ current liabilities) of at least 2:1. Long-term debt should not exceed net current assets. The balance sheet must show conservative financing.

**3. Earnings Stability**
Positive earnings every year for the past 10 years. No year of losses in the past decade. Consistency matters more than current level.

**4. Dividend Record**
Uninterrupted dividend payments for at least 20 years. Dividends are a commitment — companies that maintain them through downturns have exceptional financial discipline.

**5. Earnings Growth**
At least one-third increase in per-share earnings over the past 10 years, using 3-year averages at beginning and end to smooth out individual year variations.

**6. Moderate Price-to-Earnings**
Current price should not be more than 15x average earnings of the past 3 years.

**7. Moderate Price to Assets**
Current price should not be more than 1.5x the last reported book value. If the PE is low (under 15), the price-to-book can be higher — Graham allowed the product of PE × P/B to not exceed 22.5.

## Reading Financial Statements the Graham Way

### The Balance Sheet
Graham started with the balance sheet because it anchored everything to what actually exists.

**Assets to examine closely:**
- **Cash and equivalents:** Clean. Real.
- **Receivables:** Who owes the money? Are they creditworthy? Have receivables been growing faster than sales? (Potential manipulation)
- **Inventory:** How is it valued? FIFO or LIFO? Is it actually sellable? Inventory growing faster than sales is a warning.
- **Property, Plant & Equipment:** Net of depreciation. Is depreciation adequate or is the company under-depreciating to inflate earnings?
- **Goodwill and intangibles:** Graham was deeply skeptical. These assets can disappear in an impairment writedown. Always calculate "tangible book value" — book value minus goodwill and intangibles.

**Liabilities to examine:**
- **Off-balance-sheet obligations:** Operating leases (before ASC 842), pension obligations, contingent liabilities in footnotes
- **Debt maturity schedule:** When does the debt mature? Is it manageable?
- **Debt covenants:** If covenants are violated, debt can become due immediately — hidden risk

### The Income Statement
Graham sought normalized, sustainable earnings — not reported earnings distorted by one-time items.

**Normalization:**
- Remove one-time gains and losses
- Smooth earnings over several years to account for cycle
- Be skeptical of "adjusted" earnings that remove recurring charges

**Revenue recognition:** How does the company recognize revenue? Aggressive recognition (booking revenue before earning it) inflates current earnings at the expense of future earnings.

### The Footnotes — Where the Truth Lives
Graham said the most important information in a financial filing is almost always in the footnotes — not the headline numbers.

**What to read in footnotes:**
- Accounting policy changes (changing depreciation methods inflates earnings without real change)
- Litigation and contingencies (potential liabilities not on the balance sheet)
- Related party transactions (self-dealing by management)
- Pension and benefit obligations (often understated on the balance sheet)
- Revenue recognition policies
- Off-balance-sheet arrangements

This is exactly what Meredith Whitney did in 2007 — she read the footnotes about home equity loan exposure in bank filings when no one else bothered.
