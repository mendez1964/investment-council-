## The Core Philosophy: Narrative AND Numbers

Damodaran's most important insight: **every valuation is a story expressed in numbers.**

Most analysts either tell stories without numbers (qualitative analysts) or run numbers without stories (quantitative analysts). The first group produces entertaining narratives with no rigor. The second produces precise calculations built on arbitrary assumptions. Neither approach produces reliable valuations.

Great valuation connects a specific, believable narrative about a business to financial projections that are consistent with that narrative — and checks whether the resulting numbers justify the current price.

## The DCF Framework — The Right Way to Value a Business

### Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF)

**The calculation:**
EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate) = After-tax Operating Income
+ Depreciation and Amortization
− Capital Expenditures
− Change in Non-Cash Working Capital
= **Free Cash Flow to Firm (FCFF)**

This represents the cash generated by the business available to all capital providers (both debt holders and equity holders) before debt payments.

### The Discount Rate (WACC)

**Cost of Equity (using CAPM):**
Cost of Equity = Risk-Free Rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium

- Risk-free rate: Use current 10-year Treasury yield
- Beta: Measure of systematic risk relative to the market. Available on any financial data site. Beta of 1.0 = same risk as market. Beta of 1.5 = 50% more volatile.
- Equity Risk Premium: Damodaran estimates this annually for every country — available free on his website. For the US, historically 4-6%.

**Cost of Debt:**
After-tax cost of debt = Pretax yield on debt × (1 − Tax Rate)

**WACC:**
WACC = (Equity / Total Capital) × Cost of Equity + (Debt / Total Capital) × After-tax Cost of Debt

### Terminal Value
The most sensitive and most misused component of a DCF.

**Formula:**
Terminal Value = Final Year FCFF × (1 + Terminal Growth Rate) ÷ (WACC − Terminal Growth Rate)

**Critical constraint:** The terminal growth rate CANNOT exceed the long-term GDP growth rate of the economy. A business cannot grow faster than the economy forever — it would eventually become larger than the entire economy. Damodaran typically uses 2-3% for a stable US business in perpetuity.

**Damodaran's warning:** Terminal value can represent 60-80% of a DCF's total value in a discounted cash flow model. Small changes in terminal growth rate or WACC assumptions produce enormous changes in the output. This is where many DCF models are manipulated — by using an optimistic terminal growth rate to justify any desired valuation.

### Intrinsic Value Per Share
Sum of present values of projected FCFFs
+ Terminal Value (discounted back)
= Enterprise Value
− Net Debt (Debt minus Cash)
= Equity Value
÷ Shares Outstanding
= **Intrinsic Value Per Share**

## Relative Valuation: Multiples Done Right

### Why Most Multiple Analysis Is Wrong
Most analysts use multiples incorrectly — they compare PE ratios without understanding what drives PE ratios. Damodaran's insight: **every multiple has a fundamental driver.**

### PE Ratio
**What drives it:** Growth rate and risk. A business with higher expected earnings growth and lower risk deserves a higher PE.

**The mistake:** Comparing PE ratios of two businesses without accounting for their different growth rates and risk profiles.

**The right approach:** Compare PE to the earnings growth rate (this is the PEG ratio Lynch developed, which Damodaran validates theoretically):
- PE / Expected Earnings Growth Rate = PEG Ratio
- PEG below 1.0: Potentially undervalued relative to growth
- PEG above 2.0: Premium for quality, but expensive relative to growth

### Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio
**What drives it:** Return on Equity (ROE) relative to Cost of Equity. A business that earns 20% ROE when its cost of equity is 10% deserves to trade above book value. A business that earns only 8% ROE when its cost of equity is 10% deserves to trade below book value.

**The formula (Damodaran's key insight):**
P/B = (ROE − Growth Rate) / (Cost of Equity − Growth Rate)

A business with ROE = Cost of Equity should trade at EXACTLY book value, regardless of growth rate.

**The mistake:** Saying a stock "looks cheap because it trades at book value" without checking whether its ROE justifies any premium above book.

### EV/EBITDA
**What drives it:** Reinvestment needs and growth. A business that needs to invest very little capital to grow deserves a higher EV/EBITDA. A capital-intensive business that needs massive reinvestment to grow deserves a lower multiple.

**The mistake:** Using EV/EBITDA as if it were comparable across industries with different capex needs. A software company and a steel company should never be compared on EV/EBITDA without adjusting for reinvestment differences.

### Price-to-Sales (Revenue Multiple)
**What drives it:** Operating margin potential. A business with 40% potential operating margins deserves a much higher revenue multiple than one with 5% potential margins.

**When to use it:** For early-stage growth companies with no earnings. The revenue multiple implicitly assumes a terminal profit margin — make that assumption explicit.

## Sector-Specific Valuation

### Financial Companies (Banks, Insurance)
**The problem:** For banks, "capital" is both an input (what they borrow) and an output (loans they extend). Separating the two for a DCF is essentially impossible.

**Damodaran's approach:** Use equity-based models rather than enterprise value models.
- Discount dividends or free cash flow to equity (not firm)
- Use Price-to-Book framework grounded in ROE vs. Cost of Equity
- Focus on the bank's ability to generate returns above its cost of equity

### Early-Stage Growth Companies (No Earnings)
**The challenge:** No earnings, often no revenue, no comparable companies.

**Damodaran's approach:**
1. Start with a plausible total addressable market (TAM)
2. Estimate the market share the company can realistically capture in 10 years
3. Estimate the profit margin of a mature company in this industry
4. Calculate what earnings look like in year 10
5. Apply a mature company multiple to those earnings
6. Discount back to present
7. Compare to current market cap

**The discipline:** Make every assumption explicit. "The current price implies X% market share and Y% margins in 10 years — is that reasonable?" This converts a story into a testable quantitative claim.

### Commodity Companies
**The problem:** Earnings are driven by commodity prices, which are cyclical.

**Damodaran's approach:**
- Use normalized (cycle-average) commodity price assumptions, not current prices
- Calculate earnings at the mid-cycle commodity price
- Value based on normalized earnings
- The current commodity price creates above or below-normal earnings — do not capitalize abnormal earnings at a high multiple
