# Jeremy Grantham — Complete Analyst Framework

## Who He Is
Jeremy Grantham co-founded GMO (Grantham, Mayo, Van Otterloo) asset management in 1977. GMO manages over $60 billion. Grantham has correctly identified and predicted every major asset bubble of the past 50 years: Japanese stocks and real estate (1989), dot-com stocks (2000), US housing (2007), and what he called the "everything bubble" (2021). He publishes quarterly letters at gmo.com — all free — that are among the most intellectually serious investment writing available.

---

## The Core Philosophy: Everything Reverts to Mean

Grantham's most fundamental belief: asset prices, profit margins, and economic metrics that deviate significantly from their long-term historical averages will eventually revert to those averages — or beyond them.

**The mathematical reality:** Over very long periods (decades), mean reversion is one of the most consistent phenomena in finance. Profit margins that are historically high attract competition that drives them lower. Asset prices that are historically expensive produce lower future returns. Asset prices that are historically cheap produce higher future returns.

**The timing problem:** Grantham acknowledges he cannot time exactly when reversion will occur. He can say with confidence that extended deviations from historical norms will correct — but "eventually" can mean years or decades. This is why he emphasizes being early rather than trying to nail the exact turn.

## Bubble Identification — The Statistical Method

Grantham defines a bubble precisely and statistically: **a two standard deviation move in an asset price away from its long-term trend.**

### Why Statistical Definition Matters
By defining bubbles statistically, Grantham removes the subjective judgment of "seems expensive." Instead he asks: How far is current price from the historical trend, measured in standard deviations?

**Historical context:** Grantham has studied asset price data going back over 100 years across dozens of countries. In that data, every two standard deviation move has eventually reverted. Not most of them. Not nearly all of them. **Every single one.**

This is the empirical foundation of his entire framework: the historical record of mean reversion for extreme deviations is 100%. This does not mean it will happen quickly. It means it will happen.

### The Shiller CAPE Ratio — His Primary Valuation Tool
The Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings ratio (also called PE10) uses inflation-adjusted earnings averaged over 10 years rather than the last 12 months.

**Why 10-year average:** Single-year earnings are distorted by booms and busts. Averaging over 10 years gives a more stable picture of true earnings power through a cycle.

**Historical average:** The CAPE ratio has averaged approximately 17 over 150 years of US stock market history.

**Grantham's observations:**
- When CAPE is above 25: Returns over the next decade tend to be below average
- When CAPE is above 30: Returns tend to be poor
- When CAPE reaches 35-40: Bubble territory — historically extreme
- At the January 2022 peak: CAPE was approximately 38-40, second highest in history (1929 was similar, 2000 dot-com peak was even higher at 44)

### Tobin's Q Ratio
Another valuation measure Grantham uses: the total market value of all US corporations divided by the replacement cost of their assets.

**A Q ratio of 1.0** means the market values companies at exactly what it would cost to build them from scratch.
**Q ratio above 1.0:** Market is paying a premium over replacement cost — implies either superior profitability (justified) or overvaluation (dangerous).
**Historical average:** Approximately 0.7 — the market has historically valued companies below replacement cost on average.

## The Behavioral Characteristics of Bubbles

Grantham identifies specific behavioral signs that confirm a statistical bubble is forming:

### 1. The Parabolic Final Phase
The last 6-18 months of a bubble typically see an acceleration in price appreciation. The move that took 5 years to build doubles again in the final year. The slope of the price chart becomes nearly vertical.

**Example:** Bitcoin's 2020-2021 run accelerated dramatically in its final months before the January 2022 peak. The Nasdaq in 1999-2000 went nearly parabolic before the crash.

### 2. Narrow Leadership
As a bubble matures, fewer and fewer assets participate in the gains. In the 2021 bubble, a handful of mega-cap tech stocks accounted for the majority of index returns. When breadth narrows, the bubble is maturing.

### 3. Retail Investor Euphoria
Widespread retail participation and excitement about investing. Everyone talking about stocks at dinner parties. New brokerage accounts opening at record rates. Options trading by inexperienced investors surging. IPOs of companies with no revenue selling at extreme multiples. This retail participation is both a sign and a fueling mechanism.

### 4. New Valuation Metrics
Every bubble invents new metrics to justify prices that traditional metrics condemn as expensive. In 2000 it was "eyeballs" and "price to revenue" (because earnings did not exist). In the 1990s Japan bubble it was the "unique nature of Japanese land." In 2021 it was NFT "value" and crypto "total addressable market." When new metrics are invented to justify current prices, suspect that the old metrics were abandoned because they were inconvenient.

### 5. "This Time Is Different"
The four most dangerous words in investing, according to John Templeton. When educated, intelligent people explain why historical valuation extremes no longer apply — why THIS bubble is not a bubble — you are near the top.

### 6. Experienced Value Investors Are Dismissed
When investors with long track records of success are publicly dismissed as "dinosaurs" or "not understanding the new paradigm," the bubble is mature. This happened to Buffett in 1999-2000 (he refused to buy tech stocks and was called out of touch), to GMO in 1998-2000 (clients pulled billions as Grantham warned of the dot-com bubble), and to similar voices in every preceding bubble.

## The Post-Bubble Prediction: Reversion Often Goes Through Mean

This is perhaps the most uncomfortable part of Grantham's framework: **bubbles do not just correct to fair value — they typically overshoot below fair value.**

**The historical pattern:** After a bubble bursts, the Shiller CAPE does not just fall to 17 (the historical average). It falls to 12 or 10 or even 7. The same investor psychology that drove prices too high (greed, FOMO) then drives them too low (fear, capitulation).

**Practical implication:** If you correctly identify a bubble and expect reversion to fair value, you may still be too early to buy at what "seems cheap" after the initial correction. True bottoms in major bubbles require patience.

**Exception:** The US post-2009 recovery did not see CAPE fall below historical average before recovering — possibly due to unprecedented Fed intervention. This is the strongest counter-example to Grantham's typical pattern.

## Grantham on Climate Change as Investment Risk

He has increasingly focused on climate change as a multi-decade investment risk with specific, investable implications:

- **Agricultural commodity prices:** Increasingly volatile and biased upward as weather patterns shift
- **Transition metals:** Electric vehicle and clean energy transition requires massive amounts of copper, lithium, cobalt, nickel
- **Fossil fuel assets:** At risk of becoming stranded assets as energy transition accelerates
- **Water scarcity:** Physical water rights and water technology companies

These are very long-term themes (decades) but with near-term entry points in the commodities and technologies involved.

## The Grantham Checklist

1. Where does the Shiller CAPE stand relative to its 150-year historical average of ~17?
2. Is the current asset class showing the parabolic acceleration typical of the late bubble phase?
3. Are new valuation metrics being invented to justify current prices?
4. Is retail participation at unusual levels? Are IPOs pricing at extreme multiples?
5. Are experienced value investors being dismissed as out of touch?
6. What would it take to make this asset cheap on historical metrics? How severe must the correction be?
7. If this is indeed a bubble, what are the typical overshoot patterns in the reversion?
8. What is my time horizon — and is mean reversion likely within that time horizon?
