# Henry Blodget — Complete Analyst Framework

## Who He Is
Henry Blodget was Merrill Lynch's lead internet analyst during the dot-com era. In 1998 he issued a $400 price target on Amazon when it was trading at $240 — the stock hit it within weeks. He became the most famous analyst on Wall Street. In 2003 he was permanently banned from the securities industry by the SEC for issuing optimistic public ratings while privately calling the same stocks "pieces of junk" in emails. He later founded Business Insider, which became one of the most widely-read financial media companies in the world. His trajectory from hype to penalty to media founder gives him a uniquely complete perspective on market cycles and narrative dynamics.

## Framework 1: How Market Narratives Drive Prices

### The Narrative-Price Loop
Blodget observed firsthand how narratives drive prices — and how prices validate narratives — in a reflexive loop identical to what Soros described theoretically.

**In the dot-com era:**
1. A compelling narrative emerged: "The internet will transform every industry"
2. Early investors in internet companies made enormous returns
3. Returns attracted media attention
4. Media attention spread the narrative to more investors
5. More investors buying drove prices higher
6. Rising prices validated the narrative ("see — the market agrees")
7. Analysts (including Blodget) issued higher and higher price targets based on the narrative
8. Higher analyst targets attracted more investors
9. Until reality could no longer support the narrative and the loop reversed violently

**The lesson:** Understand whether a price movement is driven by improving fundamentals or by an escalating narrative. Narratives can be self-reinforcing for years. But they eventually collide with reality.

### Identifying Where the Narrative Is in Its Cycle

**Early narrative (when Blodget issued his Amazon call):**
- Few people believe the story yet
- The actual fundamental opportunity is real even if hard to quantify
- The stock is accessible — price has not been driven into bubble territory by the narrative
- This is when the money is made

**Mature narrative:**
- Everyone has heard the story
- Valuations are already extreme — they are "priced for perfection"
- New investors buying are paying for future certainty, not uncertainty
- Any disappointment reverses the narrative

**Dying narrative:**
- Reality has begun contradicting the story
- True believers rationalize each negative data point
- Smart money has quietly exited
- The audience left holding the bag is retail investors who bought the narrative at the top

## Framework 2: When Traditional Metrics Fail — And When They Don't

### Amazon in 1998 — Traditional Metrics Were Wrong
Amazon had no earnings. Its PE ratio was infinite. By every traditional Graham metric it was unspeakably expensive. But Blodget issued a $400 target.

**His reasoning:** Amazon was building something genuinely new — the infrastructure for internet retail. The addressable market was enormous. The unit economics of individual orders were improving rapidly. The question was not "is it profitable today" — it was "can it dominate a massive market and generate enormous earnings eventually?"

He was right. Anyone who applied strict Graham analysis and said "no earnings, cannot invest" missed one of the greatest investments in history.

**The lesson:** For genuinely transformative businesses in their early stages, traditional earnings-based metrics are the wrong tool. The right question is: What does the market look like when this company reaches maturity, and is the current price a reasonable payment for a realistic claim on that future?

### When Traditional Metrics SHOULD Override the Narrative
Blodget's blind spot — and the source of his career's end — was failing to recognize when the narrative had detached from any plausible future reality.

By 1999-2000, internet stocks were not being priced for realistic success scenarios. They were being priced for scenarios that assumed:
- Every internet company would dominate
- None would face significant competition
- Growth rates seen in first-year startups would continue for decades
- Traditional business economics did not apply

When the gap between price and any realistic fundamental scenario becomes extreme, traditional metrics become the anchor to reality — even if they are temporarily irrelevant. The investor's job is to determine WHICH phase applies: "traditional metrics are wrong because this is transformative" or "traditional metrics are right and the narrative has detached from reality."

## Framework 3: Media as a Sentiment Indicator

Blodget now leads Business Insider and understands better than almost anyone how financial media shapes and reflects investor sentiment.

**Stories drive flows, flows drive prices — temporarily:**
When financial media creates a positive narrative around a stock or sector, it attracts more attention, more investor interest, and higher prices. But the underlying business determines whether prices hold.

**Media coverage as a cycle indicator:**
- No media coverage: Very early stage, opportunity exists for those who do their own research
- Positive mainstream media coverage beginning: Opportunity narrowing but still real
- Cover of major magazines/publications: Typically a contrary indicator (tops)
- "The Death of X" or "X is over" coverage: Typically a contrary indicator (bottoms)

**The Business Week Curse / Magazine Cover Indicator:** The classic contrarian indicator. When a trend or asset appears on the cover of a major magazine, the trend is usually near its end. The media only covers trends after they have become obvious — by definition, that is after most of the opportunity has been captured.

## The Blodget Checklist

1. Is the narrative driving this stock price early (real opportunity) or late (everyone already knows the story)?
2. Is the current price predicated on genuine transformation potential or simply narrative extension?
3. How much are traditional metrics (PE, P/S) diverging from historical norms? Is the divergence justified by genuine disruption or inflated narrative?
4. What does financial media coverage look like — early and skeptical (opportunity) or enthusiastic and ubiquitous (risk)?
5. Are insiders buying (believe in the story) or selling (cashing out before the narrative fades)?
6. At current valuation, what must go RIGHT for the investment to work? Is that realistic?
7. What would make the narrative die? How likely is that scenario?
