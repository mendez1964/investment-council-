# Paul Tudor Jones Framework — Complete Philosophy

## Core Identity
Paul Tudor Jones founded Tudor Investment Corporation in 1980. Has generated consistent returns for over 40 years. Best known for predicting and profiting from the 1987 stock market crash — his fund returned 62% in October 1987 when the market fell 22% in one day. He is the master of risk management, trend following, and macro trading. His famous rule: 5 to 1 reward-to-risk minimum on every trade.

## The Central Philosophy in One Sentence
Capital preservation comes first, always — if you protect your capital, the opportunities to make money will come to you; if you lose your capital, nothing else matters.

---

## The Foundation: Risk Management Above Everything Else

Tudor Jones says the most important thing he learned is that a great trader is first and foremost a defensive player. Offense is secondary.

### The 5-to-1 Rule
Every trade must offer a minimum 5-to-1 reward-to-risk ratio. This means:
- If the stop loss is $1 away from entry, the profit target must be $5 away or more
- If the stop loss is 5%, the price target must be at least 25%
- If the stop loss is $500, the expected gain must be at least $2,500

**Why this ratio matters mathematically:** If you only win 30% of your trades but maintain 5-to-1 reward-to-risk, you are still profitable. You can be wrong the majority of the time and still make money. This is the key to surviving long enough to get good.

### The Stop Loss Is Sacred
Tudor Jones places his stop loss BEFORE he enters a trade. Never after. The stop is not adjustable based on hope or emotion — it is the price where the trade thesis is wrong and he exits. Period.

**His rules on stops:**
- Never move a stop loss further away to "give the trade more room" — this is the most common way traders blow up accounts
- Move stops in the direction of the trade as it works — trailing stops lock in gains
- When price hits the stop, exit immediately — do not hesitate, do not think, do not renegotiate with yourself
- The stop loss is an insurance policy. You pay the premium (small loss) to protect against catastrophe (large loss)

### Never Average Down Into a Losing Position
This is perhaps his most repeated rule. When a trade is going against you, adding to it is doubling down on something the market is telling you is wrong.

"Losers average losers" is his famous line.

Adding to a loser:
- Increases position size at the worst possible moment
- Raises your average entry price, making it harder to recover
- Creates emotional attachment to being right rather than making money
- Can turn a manageable loss into an account-destroying disaster

The professional response to a losing trade: exit at the predetermined stop, reassess with fresh eyes, and only re-enter if the thesis is still valid with a new proper setup.

## Trend Following — The Primary Strategy

Tudor Jones is fundamentally a trend follower who uses macro catalysts to identify and enter the right trends.

### The Trend Analysis Process

**Step 1: Identify the trend on every relevant timeframe**

Monthly chart: What is the multi-year direction? Is this asset in a secular bull or bear market?
Weekly chart: What is the intermediate trend? Have we been making higher highs and higher lows?
Daily chart: What is the current short-to-medium term direction?
Hourly chart: What is the immediate trend? Is price currently rising or falling?

The strongest trades occur when all timeframes align — when the monthly, weekly, daily, and hourly are all moving in the same direction. This is what Tudor Jones calls "the trend is your friend."

**Step 2: Find the entry point with the best risk-reward**

In an uptrend: Look for pullbacks to key support levels to buy — not breakouts at new highs where risk is greatest
In a downtrend: Look for rallies to key resistance levels to sell — not new lows where risk is greatest

The goal is to enter when price has pulled back far enough to offer a tight stop loss but is still within the larger trend. This maximizes the reward-to-risk ratio.

**Step 3: Identify the macro catalyst that drives the trend**

Tudor Jones believes technical trends are caused by fundamental changes in the world. He uses technical analysis to time entry and exit but macro analysis to select which trends to follow.

A technical breakout without a macro catalyst behind it is much weaker than one with a powerful macro tailwind.

## Macro Analysis — Finding the Catalyst

Tudor Jones made his 1987 crash prediction by combining technical pattern recognition with macroeconomic analysis.

**His 1987 process:**
- He studied the 1929 crash extensively and saw a similar technical pattern forming in 1987
- He identified the macro catalyst: interest rates had risen sharply, making bonds more attractive than stocks
- He saw the technical pattern and macro alignment — both pointing to a correction
- He shorted massively and made 62% in October 1987 when the market fell 22%

**Macro factors Tudor Jones monitors:**
- Interest rate trend: Rising rates are bad for stocks and bonds. Falling rates are good.
- Yield curve: When short rates rise above long rates (inverted curve), recession often follows
- Dollar trend: Strong dollar hurts commodities and emerging markets. Weak dollar helps both.
- Commodity trends: Rising oil and materials signal inflation. Falling signals deflation.
- Credit spreads: When high yield spreads widen, risk appetite is declining — danger sign
- Global market alignment: Is the US leading or lagging other global markets?

## The Four Markets That Matter Most to Tudor Jones

### Equities
Trend follows macro cycle. When the Fed is cutting and economy is recovering, buy stocks. When Fed is hiking and economy is late cycle, reduce or short. Uses technical patterns for precise entry and exit.

### Fixed Income (Bonds)
Bonds move inversely to interest rates. When rates fall, bond prices rise. One of his best trades historically. Understands that bonds are a macro asset driven by central bank policy and inflation expectations.

### Currencies
Very active currency trader. Currencies trend strongly and for long periods when macro forces are powerful. Follows purchasing power parity, interest rate differentials, and current account dynamics.

### Commodities
Fundamental supply-demand drives long-term trends. Futures allow leverage and precise trend following. Understands seasonal patterns, supply chain analysis, and global demand shifts.

## Position Sizing — The Science of Bet Sizing

Tudor Jones thinks carefully about how much to risk on any given trade.

**His framework:**
- Never risk more than 1-2% of total capital on any single trade
- In a drawdown, reduce position size — when losing, bet smaller
- In a winning streak, do not automatically increase size — stay disciplined
- Maximum portfolio heat (total capital at risk across all positions): 6-8%
- If you lose more than a certain amount in a month, stop trading for the rest of the month

**The losing streak protocol:**
After 3 consecutive losing trades, he cuts position sizes in half. The market is telling him something is wrong with his read on the environment. The answer is not to fight back harder — it is to get smaller, reassess, and wait for clarity to return.

**The winning streak protocol:**
After a strong run, he does not get overconfident and increase size dramatically. He has seen too many traders give back months of gains in a few days of over-sized trading after a good run made them feel invincible.

## Technical Analysis — His Toolset

Tudor Jones uses several technical tools consistently:

**Moving averages:** He famously loves the 200-day moving average as the bull/bear dividing line. If price is above the 200-day MA, he leans bullish. Below, he leans bearish. Crossing the 200-day in either direction is a significant signal.

**Momentum:** He does not fight strong momentum. When a market is in a powerful trend, he does not look for the top or bottom — he trades with the momentum until the trend shows exhaustion signals.

**Volume:** He wants to see volume confirm price moves. A breakout on declining volume is suspect. A breakout on surging volume is legitimate.

**Historical patterns:** He is famous for studying historical chart patterns extensively. He once overlaid the 1987 chart on the 1929 chart and saw the eerie similarity before Black Monday. He constantly looks for pattern repetition across history.

**Fibonacci levels:** Uses them for identifying potential support and resistance levels where price might reverse or pause.

## The Psychological Foundation

Tudor Jones is extremely self-aware about the psychological challenges of trading. He has said:

"The secret to being successful from a trading perspective is to have an indefatigable and an undying and unquenchable thirst for information and knowledge."

"Every day I assume every position I have is wrong."

This last quote is crucial. He starts every day questioning whether his existing positions are still valid. He does not let conviction become attachment. He is willing to be wrong today even about something he was right about yesterday.

**His emotional management rules:**
- Never make a trading decision based on what you need to happen — make it based on what you see
- Do not get attached to any position — when the trade is wrong, the trade is wrong
- Do not let a loss make you try to "make it back" in the same day — this is revenge trading
- Do not let a win make you reckless — markets do not know or care what you made last week

## Tudor Jones on Crypto

He has been one of the most high-profile institutional investors in Bitcoin.

**His thesis (stated publicly in 2020):**
- Inflation is coming due to unprecedented money printing by central banks globally
- Bitcoin is the fastest horse in the inflation race — a hard asset with a fixed supply
- He allocated approximately 2% of his portfolio to Bitcoin as an inflation hedge
- He views Bitcoin as digital gold — a store of value, not a payment system

**His sizing approach:** He applied his typical risk management framework. Small enough that being completely wrong (Bitcoin goes to zero) does not damage his overall portfolio. Large enough that being right provides meaningful upside.

This is the Tudor Jones approach applied to crypto: size it appropriately for the uncertainty, manage the stop, treat it as a macro asset in the inflation trade.

## When to Apply the Tudor Jones Framework

**Use Tudor Jones for:**
- Entry and exit timing on any trade in any asset class
- Stop loss placement — where does the trade thesis become wrong?
- Position sizing — how much of your capital to risk
- Identifying the primary trend and aligning trades with it
- Macro catalyst identification for trend trades
- Managing psychology during losing streaks
- Any momentum or trend-following opportunity

**Do not use Tudor Jones for:**
- Selecting businesses for long-term fundamental investment — wrong tool
- Value analysis — he does not buy things because they are cheap
- When there is no clear trend — he waits for clarity

## The Tudor Jones Checklist

1. What is the trend on the monthly, weekly, daily, and hourly chart — do they align?
2. What is the macro catalyst driving this trend?
3. Where is the precise entry point that minimizes risk?
4. Where exactly does the trade thesis become wrong? (This is your stop loss)
5. What is the realistic profit target?
6. What is the reward-to-risk ratio — is it at least 5-to-1?
7. How much of my total account am I risking? (Should be 1-2% maximum)
8. Am I averaging down into an existing loser? (Never do this)
9. Is momentum supporting the direction of my trade?
10. Am I trading my plan or my emotions right now?
