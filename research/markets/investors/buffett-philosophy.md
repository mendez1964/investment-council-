# Warren Buffett Framework — Complete Philosophy

## Core Identity
Warren Buffett is the greatest long-term investor in history. Chairman of Berkshire Hathaway. Student of Benjamin Graham who then evolved beyond Graham's pure statistical approach into quality business analysis. His partner Charlie Munger pushed him toward paying fair prices for wonderful businesses rather than bargain prices for mediocre ones. 78 years of investing. Never lost money over any 10-year period.

## The Central Philosophy in One Sentence
Buy wonderful businesses at fair prices, run by honest and capable people, and hold them essentially forever.

## The Four Filters — Every Investment Must Pass All Four

### Filter 1: Can I Understand This Business?
Buffett calls this his "circle of competence." He will not invest in what he does not understand deeply.
- He must be able to explain how the business makes money in one simple paragraph
- He must understand what could go wrong and why
- He must be able to project earnings 10 years out with reasonable confidence
- If he cannot do these things, the answer is always no regardless of how cheap it looks
- Famous example: He did not invest in tech stocks in the 1990s boom. Was called out of touch. Avoided the entire dot-com crash.

**Questions to ask:** Can you explain this business to a smart 12-year-old? Can you name the three biggest risks to the business model? Can you estimate earnings in 10 years within a reasonable range?

### Filter 2: Does the Business Have a Durable Competitive Advantage?
Buffett calls this an "economic moat." A moat is what prevents competitors from taking your customers.

**Types of moats Buffett recognizes:**

*Brand moat:* Customers pay more because of the name. Coca-Cola, See's Candies, Apple. People buy without comparing prices. The brand is the product.

*Switching cost moat:* Customers are locked in because changing is too painful or expensive. American Express, Moody's, Wells Fargo banking relationships. Once embedded, customers stay for decades.

*Network effect moat:* The product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Visa, Mastercard. Every new card user makes the network more valuable to merchants and vice versa.

*Low-cost moat:* The business can produce goods or services cheaper than any competitor can possibly match. GEICO insurance. Their direct-to-consumer model eliminates agent commissions — a structural cost advantage.

*Toll bridge moat:* The business controls something essential that everyone must use. Burlington Northern railroad — if you want to ship goods across certain corridors, you use their tracks. There is no other option.

**The moat durability test:** Will this competitive advantage still exist in 20 years? What would destroy it? Buffett sold his airline positions because airlines have no durable moat — any competitor can copy your product and undercut your price.

### Filter 3: Is Management Honest and Capable?
Buffett says he looks for three things in people: intelligence, energy, and integrity. And if they don't have the third, the first two will kill you.

**Signs of honest management:**
- They communicate bad news quickly and clearly — not buried in footnotes
- They set realistic guidance and meet it rather than playing games to beat by a penny
- Their compensation is aligned with shareholders — skin in the game
- They use shareholder capital carefully — not wasting money on acquisitions to grow their empire
- They buy back stock when it is cheap, not just to hit EPS targets
- They write to shareholders like partners, not customers

**Signs of questionable management:**
- Constant use of "adjusted earnings" that exclude real costs
- Acquisitions that destroy value but increase executive compensation
- Guidance that is always suspiciously optimistic
- Excessive stock options diluting shareholders
- CEO who talks about stock price rather than business performance

**Buffett's test:** Would I be comfortable if this person managed my personal life savings with no supervision? Would I be proud to own this company regardless of what anyone else thinks?

### Filter 4: Is the Price Reasonable Relative to Intrinsic Value?
Buffett calculates intrinsic value as the discounted present value of all cash the business will generate over its remaining life. He does not use precise formulas — he uses judgment.

**What he pays attention to:**
- Owner earnings: Net income plus depreciation minus maintenance capital expenditure. This is real cash the business generates for owners.
- Free cash flow yield: What percentage of the purchase price does the business earn in free cash flow each year?
- Return on equity: Is the business generating high returns on the capital it employs? Above 15% consistently is excellent.
- Earnings power relative to price: He thinks of buying a business like buying a bond — what is the earnings yield?

**The margin of safety:** Buffett inherited this concept from Graham. Never pay full price. If intrinsic value is $100, pay $70 or less. The gap between what you pay and what it is worth protects you from being wrong about your analysis.

**Buffett's famous quote on price:** "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."

## Mr. Market Psychology — The Most Important Mental Model

Benjamin Graham invented the concept. Buffett made it the cornerstone of his entire philosophy.

Imagine you own a business with a partner called Mr. Market. Every single day Mr. Market knocks on your door and offers to buy your share of the business or sell you his share at a specific price.

Some days Mr. Market is euphoric. He thinks the business is worth a fortune and offers you a very high price. Some days Mr. Market is terrified. He thinks the business is worth very little and offers you a bargain price.

The crucial insight: Mr. Market's daily price quotations have nothing to do with the actual value of the business. The business does not become worth less when Mr. Market is scared. It does not become worth more when he is excited.

**The intelligent investor's response:** Ignore Mr. Market's mood entirely. Use him only when he is being irrational in your favor — buy from him when he is terrified and selling cheap, sell to him when he is euphoric and paying too much. Never let his daily price quotes influence your view of what you own.

**This is why Buffett loves market crashes:** Lower prices on wonderful businesses he wants to own. He calls market crashes "sales at the department store." Everyone else panics and sells. Buffett buys.

## What Buffett Never Does

- Invests in businesses he does not understand
- Tries to time the market
- Pays attention to macroeconomic forecasts
- Sells a wonderful business because the price dropped
- Buys a mediocre business because it is cheap
- Diversifies into things he is not confident about
- Uses leverage to amplify returns
- Acts on tips, rumors, or analyst recommendations
- Sells because of short-term earnings disappointments

## Buffett on Different Asset Classes

**Stocks:** His preferred asset. Ownership in businesses that compound value over time. Holds forever if the business remains excellent.

**Bonds:** Acceptable in specific situations but generally inferior to stocks over long periods because they do not share in business growth.

**Gold:** He famously dislikes gold. It produces nothing. It earns nothing. It is only worth what someone will pay for it in the future. A non-productive asset.

**Crypto:** Deep skepticism. Does not produce anything. Has no intrinsic value based on earnings or assets. Buffett famously called Bitcoin "rat poison squared." He acknowledges he may be wrong but crypto violates everything he believes about value.

**Real estate:** Respects it as a productive asset. Has owned real estate through Berkshire. Applies same principles — what does it earn, what is a fair price, is it durable.

## When to Apply the Buffett Framework

**Use Buffett when:**
- Evaluating whether a business is worth owning at all
- Deciding between two investment options for long-term capital
- Determining what to do when a stock you own drops significantly
- Assessing whether management deserves your trust
- Valuing any established business with predictable earnings

**Do not use Buffett for:**
- Timing entries and exits — he ignores timing entirely
- Crypto analysis — his framework does not apply to non-productive assets
- Short-term trading — he has zero interest in this
- High-growth speculative companies with no earnings — his tools do not work on these

## The Buffett Checklist — Run Every Investment Through This

1. Do I understand exactly how this business makes money?
2. Has this business earned consistently high returns on equity for 10+ years?
3. Does the business generate more cash than it needs to run itself?
4. Is the competitive advantage durable — will it exist in 20 years?
5. Is management honest — do they communicate bad news clearly?
6. Is management capable — have they allocated capital well historically?
7. Is the price I am paying reasonable relative to normalized earnings power?
8. Am I buying with an adequate margin of safety?
9. Would I be comfortable owning this if the market closed for 5 years?
10. Is this business within my circle of competence?

If any answer is no — pass. Wait for the next pitch. Buffett famously says the stock market is the only place where when things go on sale, people run out of the store.
