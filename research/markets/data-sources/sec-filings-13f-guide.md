# SEC Filings and 13F Data — Complete Guide

## Where to Find SEC Filings

### SEC EDGAR — The Primary Source
**URL:** sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar

All public company filings are here. Free. Updated in real-time as filings are submitted.

**How to search:**
1. Go to sec.gov
2. Click "Search" or go directly to efts.sec.gov/LATEST/search-index?q=
3. Enter company name or ticker
4. Select filing type from the dropdown
5. Click through to view filings

## Key Filing Types and What They Tell You

### 10-K (Annual Report)
Filed once per year within 60-90 days of fiscal year end.

**This is the most important document for any fundamental analysis.** It contains:
- Complete audited financial statements (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow)
- Management Discussion and Analysis (MD&A) — management's explanation of results
- Risk factors — required disclosure of all material risks
- Business description — how the company actually makes money
- **FOOTNOTES** — where all the real details live (see Whitney framework)

**How to read it efficiently:**
1. Start with the Risk Factors section — understand the bears' best arguments
2. Read the MD&A — management's narrative about the business
3. Read the financial statements
4. Read the footnotes carefully (revenue recognition, debt terms, contingencies)
5. Compare to prior year 10-K — what changed?

### 10-Q (Quarterly Report)
Filed three times per year (the 4th quarter is covered by the 10-K). Due within 40-45 days of quarter end.

Unaudited but follows the same structure as the 10-K. Shows quarterly progress. Most investors focus on the 10-Q revenue and earnings but the real insights are in the management commentary and footnotes.

### 8-K (Material Events)
Filed within 4 business days of any "material event." This includes:
- Earnings releases
- Major acquisitions or divestitures
- CEO or CFO changes
- Going-concern warnings
- Restatements of prior earnings
- Credit agreement changes
- Any event that a reasonable investor would want to know about

**8-K alert strategy:** Set up email alerts for specific companies on SEC EDGAR → "Filing Activity Alerts." When a company you follow files an 8-K, you get notified immediately.

### Form 4 (Insider Transactions)
Filed within 2 business days of any insider transaction.

**The most actionable information:**
- **Open market purchase (code P):** Executive spent their own money buying stock. Most bullish signal.
- **Open market sale (code S):** Executive sold stock. Less meaningful (many reasons to sell).
- **Option exercise (code M):** Exercise of previously granted options. Often followed by sale. Less meaningful.

**The cluster buy signal:** When multiple executives (CEO, CFO, multiple directors) all buy stock in the open market within the same week, this is an exceptional signal. Independent people with full knowledge of the company are all putting personal money in at current prices. This is the closest thing to insider information that is perfectly legal to act on.

**Where to find Form 4:** SEC EDGAR search, GuruFocus.com (free with delay), or OpenInsider.com (free, fast UI for insider tracking).

### 13D and 13G (Large Ownership Disclosures)
Any investor acquiring 5% or more of a public company's outstanding shares must file:
- **13D:** Investor intends to be an active participant in company governance (activist)
- **13G:** Passive investor (mutual fund, index fund)

**The 13D signal:** When a known activist fund (Ackman, Icahn, Elliott, Starboard) files a 13D, the stock immediately pops. The market knows: management is about to face demands for change. Often a catalyst for significant price appreciation.

**How to track 13D filings:** SEC EDGAR email alerts, WhaleWisdom.com has a 13D tracker.

## 13F Analysis — Tracking Hedge Fund Positions

### What 13F Shows
Every investment manager with over $100 million in 13F-reportable securities must file within 45 days of each quarter end. Shows all long US equity positions and options (calls and puts held).

### What 13F Does NOT Show
- Short positions (hedge funds do not have to disclose shorts)
- International stock positions
- Bond positions
- Futures positions
- Any position completely exited before quarter end
- New positions initiated after the quarter end date

**The critical limitation:** You are seeing stale data. A 13F filed in mid-May shows positions as of March 31. The fund may have already exited everything. Use 13F data for understanding *investment thesis and style*, not for front-running specific trades.

## Practical 13F Trading Strategy

### New Position Detection
When a respected investor shows a brand new position in their 13F that didn't appear in the prior quarter, that stock deserves immediate investigation. They identified the opportunity fresh — not a legacy position, not averaging down. New conviction.

**Process:**
1. Get 13F change alerts from WhaleWisdom
2. When a new position appears from a respected fund, pull up the stock immediately
3. Look at the chart — where did they likely buy? What has it done since?
4. Read the fundamental story — why would this fund own it?
5. If the thesis is still intact and price hasn't run away, it may be worth investigating further

### Understanding Investment Style
13F data tells you how a manager thinks. After reviewing several quarters of holdings, you understand:
- What sectors they favor
- What their average holding period is
- Whether they run concentrated or diversified portfolios
- Whether they are value, growth, or quality focused
- What market cap range they target

This helps you evaluate whether their new positions fit their established pattern or represent something unusual.
