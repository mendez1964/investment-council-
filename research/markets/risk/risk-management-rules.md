# Risk Management Rules — The Foundation of Everything

## The Most Important Section in the Entire Knowledge Base

Every framework in the Investment Council — Buffett, Dalio, Soros, Tudor Jones, Livermore, all of them — assumes one thing as a prerequisite: you survive long enough to be right.

Risk management is not a secondary concern. It is the primary discipline. Paul Tudor Jones has said he considers himself a defensive player first. Livermore went bankrupt four times by abandoning his own risk rules. The traders who last decades have one thing in common — they do not let any single trade destroy them.

---

## The Universal Rules — Non-Negotiable

### Rule 1: Define Maximum Risk Per Trade — 1-2% of Total Account
Never risk more than 1-2% of your total account value on any single trade.

**What this means practically:**
- $50,000 account: Maximum risk per trade = $500 to $1,000
- $100,000 account: Maximum risk per trade = $1,000 to $2,000
- $25,000 account: Maximum risk per trade = $250 to $500

**This is not the position size — this is the dollar amount you can lose if the trade hits your stop.**

If you have a $50,000 account and risk 2% ($1,000), and your stop is $2 away from your entry, you can own 500 shares.

### Rule 2: Always Define the Stop Loss Before Entering
Before you enter any trade, know exactly:
- Where your stop loss is
- How much money you lose if stopped out
- Whether that amount is within your 1-2% risk rule

If you cannot define where the trade is wrong before entering, you do not have a trade — you have a hope.

### Rule 3: Minimum 2-to-1 Reward-to-Risk
For every trade you take, the potential profit must be at least 2x the potential loss. Tudor Jones requires 5-to-1. Start with 2-to-1 minimum.

**Why this math works:** If you take only 2-to-1 trades and you are right just 40% of the time:
- 4 winners × $200 = $800
- 6 losers × $100 = -$600
- Net result: +$200 profit while being wrong 60% of the time

**Never accept a trade where the profit target is smaller than the stop loss.**

### Rule 4: Never Average Down Into a Losing Position
Livermore called this the most important rule. Tudor Jones echoes it. When a trade is going against you, do not add to it to lower your average price.

**Why averaging down destroys accounts:**
- The trade is telling you the thesis is wrong
- Adding more size at lower prices increases your total loss when stopped
- It creates emotional attachment to the trade (sunk cost fallacy)
- It can turn a 2% loss into a 10% or 20% loss

**The rule:** If a trade is going against you, the only acceptable responses are:
1. Hold if price has not hit your stop and the thesis is still intact
2. Exit at your predetermined stop

Adding is never acceptable.

### Rule 5: Maximum Portfolio Heat — 6-8%
Total capital at risk across ALL open positions simultaneously should not exceed 6-8% of your account.

**Example:** $100,000 account. You have 3 open trades, each risking $2,000 (2%). Total exposure = $6,000 = 6%. If a black swan event hits and all 3 trades stop out simultaneously, you lose 6%.

This is manageable. 6% is recoverable. 40% is a career-ending event.

**The correlation problem:** Do not stack correlated trades. Three long tech stocks look like 3 positions but behave like 1 trade — they all move together. True diversification means your positions are uncorrelated.

## Position Sizing Formula

This formula determines exactly how many shares to buy:

**Position Size = Dollar Risk ÷ Distance to Stop**

**Example:**
- Account: $100,000
- Maximum risk: 2% = $2,000
- Stock price: $50
- Stop loss: $47 (3 points away)
- Position size: $2,000 ÷ $3 = 666 shares
- Dollar value of position: 666 × $50 = $33,300 (33% of account in this one position)

This is fine — the position is large but the *risk* is controlled at 2%.

**Never size based on "how much you can afford to buy." Always size based on how much you can afford to lose.**

## Drawdown Management

### The Three Losing Trades Protocol
After 3 consecutive losing trades, cut position size in half.

**Why:** 3 consecutive losses suggests either:
1. The overall market environment has shifted against your strategy
2. Your read on the market is currently off
3. Bad luck (but better to be safe)

Half size until you string together 2-3 winners. Then return to normal size. This prevents a losing streak from compounding into a devastating drawdown.

### Daily Maximum Loss Limit
Set a maximum dollar amount you will lose in any single trading day. When you hit it, stop trading for the day.

**Common rules:**
- Day traders: Stop at 3% of account loss in one day
- Swing traders: Less relevant since you hold positions

**Why it matters:** Bad days produce emotional decisions. Emotional decisions produce worse trades. The worst losing streaks happen when traders try to "make it back" after a bad day. The loss multiplies. Stop. Walk away. Tomorrow is a fresh start.

### Weekly Review Process
Every week, review:
- Total P&L for the week
- Win rate (% of trades that were winners)
- Average winner vs average loser (reward-to-risk achieved)
- Any rule violations — did you move a stop? Average down? Hold through a pre-announced risk event?
- What would you do differently?

The review is how you improve. Traders who do not review their trades do not improve — they just repeat the same mistakes.

## Specific Risk Rules by Asset Class

### Stocks
- Avoid holding through earnings unless that is the explicit strategy
- Do not hold through major macro events (FOMC, CPI) unless you understand the risk
- Be aware of liquidity — small cap stocks can gap through stops
- Know the short interest — high short interest creates both opportunity (squeeze) and risk (stocks with huge short interest are being shorted by sophisticated players who know something)

### Options
- Never risk more than you are willing to lose to zero — options can expire worthless
- Understand theta decay — time is always working against the buyer
- Never buy options with earnings if you have not studied IV crush
- Size options positions smaller than stock positions — the leverage cuts both ways
- Spreads (buying one option, selling another) limit both upside and downside — useful for defined-risk setups

### Crypto
- Position size smaller than stocks due to higher volatility
- 24/7 market means moves happen while you sleep — use stop losses on exchanges
- Correlation risk — most altcoins move with Bitcoin; owning 5 altcoins is not diversification
- Liquidity risk — smaller tokens can be manipulated and have wide spreads
- Custody risk — not your keys, not your coins (exchange counterparty risk)

### Futures
- Understand the leverage — a standard S&P 500 futures contract controls $250,000+ of stock with $10,000 margin
- A 1% move in the index is a 25% move against your margin
- Use micro contracts until you are consistently profitable
- Understand rollover dates and the cost of carry

## The Psychology of Loss

### Why Losses Feel Disproportionately Painful
Behavioral economists (Kahneman, Tversky) have shown that losses feel approximately 2-2.5x more painful than equivalent gains feel pleasurable. This asymmetry causes traders to:
- Hold losses too long (hoping they reverse to avoid feeling the pain)
- Cut winners too early (grabbing pleasure before it can reverse)
- This is precisely backward from what works

**The solution:** Process-based evaluation. Was the trade managed according to the rules? If yes, a loss is acceptable — it is a cost of doing business. If no (you held past your stop, you did not take profits where you planned), the loss is a failure of process, not just of outcome.

### Accepting Small Losses as a Business Cost
Every business has costs. Options premium decay is a cost. Stop losses are costs. The question is not "how do I avoid losses" — the question is "are my wins big enough and frequent enough to cover my losses with profit left over?"

A loss taken at the predetermined stop is not a mistake. It is the system working correctly.

A loss held past the stop is always a mistake — regardless of whether it eventually recovers.
